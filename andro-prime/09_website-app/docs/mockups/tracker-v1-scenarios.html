Design challenge — 3+ points on a sparkline

Three results means the sparkline must now show a curve shape, not just a direction. A flat line with an uptick tells a different story than a steady climb. Two decisions are required: (1) the trend badge should reflect first → last for overall direction, but should also flag if the most recent reading declined vs the previous one — that's the signal the customer cares most about right now. (2) The x-axis spacing must be proportional to elapsed time, not evenly distributed — a 4-month gap followed by a 3-month gap should look different visually. Cards below show both an "improving overall" state and a "recent dip" state side by side.

Shape A — Steady climb (rate slowing)

Badge: ↑ Improving — rate is slowing but direction is positive. CTA: "still climbing — retest to confirm plateau."

Shape B — Improved then dipped (recent decline)

Badge: ↓ Declined (last vs previous, even though above starting point). Open circle = below BSSM 12 nmol/L line. Compliance note mandatory.

Even spacing — misleading

Jan→Feb (1 month) looks the same as Feb→Aug (6 months). Rate of change is visually misrepresented.

Proportional spacing — correct

Narrow Jan→Feb gap, wide Feb→Aug gap. The pace — a quick early measurement then a long plateau — is readable from the shape alone.

S6 note: Jan→May (115 days) and May→Aug (97 days) are nearly even, so the visual difference in S6's actual cards is small. The rule matters most when gaps are uneven — or at 4+ results where time compression makes a real difference.

Design rule — 4+ results: show all points, proportional spacing, badge still uses last 2

Show every result — don't clip to a fixed window. The proportional x-axis naturally compresses older points left as new tests arrive, so visual density stays manageable. The trend badge still reflects the last two readings only; the sparkline shape carries the long-term story. For 6+ tests (roughly 18 months of quarterly testing) a taller card may be needed to keep points readable — production concern, not MVP.

Design rule — when the most recent result declines

Show "↓ Declined" badge on the card regardless of whether the overall first→last direction is positive. A recent dip is the signal the customer needs to act on now. Add an inline compliance note: "A decline in one result doesn't necessarily indicate a medical issue — speak to your GP if you have concerns." Do not suppress the badge or soften it to "→ Stable" just because the value is still above the starting point.

Design recommendation — one tracker that handles all scenarios

Four marker states (not two)

The tracker needs four distinct card states: Trending (2+ results from kits that share the marker), First result (measured once, dashed future line), Not tested (greyed-out placeholder — acts as an upsell surface), and Gap (last known value shown with amber warning — marker was tested before but not in the most recent kit). Most tracker designs only handle the first two.

The gap problem is a Kit 3 conversion trigger

Scenario S3 (Kit 1 → Kit 2) is the most commercially important edge case. Two tests, nine markers measured, zero trends — because the kits don't overlap. The tracker should not hide this or smooth it over. Surface it clearly: "You have the broadest baseline possible — one Kit 3 test will trend all 9 at once." Turn the frustration into the strongest upgrade argument.

Group by trend readiness, not by kit

Group the biomarker grid by "trending / first result / not tested" — not by which kit they came from. A customer doesn't think in kits; they think in what they know and what's missing. The group header should say "Trending across 2 results" not "Kit 1 markers." This also naturally separates improving markers (the win) from first-results (the next step).

The supplement bar is always present

Even when no supplement is active, show an empty supplement track with an upsell prompt. The supplement bar is not a history widget — it is the core value proposition made visual. An active Daily Stack bar spanning the gap between two tests is the most powerful single visual in the tracker. Never hide the track; show what's possible even when it's empty.

Context-aware CTA rules

After Kit 3 first test (S1)

"[N] days of Daily Stack — retest to see what's moved." Urgency built on supplement tenure, not anxiety about the results.

After same-kit repeat with untested markers (S2)

"Your hormones are improving — your energy panel is still unmeasured." Show exactly which markers are missing. Offer Kit 3 as primary, Kit 2 as secondary.

After Kit 1 → Kit 2 with no trends (S3)

"2 tests, 0 trends — Kit 3 solves this." The most direct, most urgent CTA in the system. Do not soften it with "if you'd like" language. Make Kit 3 the obvious single action.

After Kit 1 → Kit 3 with partial trends (S4)

"[N] markers trending — [M] still need a second reading." Show the exact count. CTA is "Retest Kit 3."

After Kit 3 × 2 with all trends (S5 — north star)

Celebrate. "All 9 markers trending." CTA is soft: "Schedule next retest ~3 months." Do not introduce urgency where none exists — this customer is already in the loop.

After Kit 1 × 3+ (S6 — repeat hormone buyer)

Escalate the upsell in proportion to tenure. After 3 tests, the "not tested" group label should say "7 months unmeasured" — not just "not in Kit 1." The CTA primary action becomes Kit 3, not another Kit 1. The customer has proved commitment to testing; the conversion argument is now about completing the picture they've invested in, not starting something new.

After Kit 2 × 3+ (S7 — repeat energy buyer)

Same logic as S6, mirrored. The upsell connects hormones directly to the energy symptoms the customer is clearly trying to address: "Low hormonal health is one of the most common causes of persistent fatigue — you've never measured it." A declining marker (e.g. ferritin drop) in the most recent test also creates urgency for the next retest regardless of kit choice.

Trend badge rule with 3+ points — always show recent vs previous

The trend badge reflects the last two readings, not first→last. If testosterone was 11.2 → 14.8 → 12.1, the badge reads "↓ Declined" even though the person is above their starting point. The overall arc is shown visually in the sparkline shape. The badge answers the question the customer has right now: "is it going the right way?" The sparkline answers the broader question: "what's the full story?"

Sparkline x-axis: proportional time spacing, not even distribution

If tests are 4 months apart then 3 months apart, the visual gap between points 1→2 should be wider than 2→3. Even-distribution makes a March–April gap look the same as a January–August gap, which misrepresents the pace of change. For the MVP this can be approximate; for production it should be calculated from actual dates. This matters most when the customer has 4+ results and the sparkline starts to look like a real time series.

Threshold crossing — when a marker enters or leaves reference range

When a marker crosses the reference boundary between two tests — in either direction — the open/filled circle convention is the primary visual signal: an open circle (out of range) followed by a filled circle (in range) marks the crossing. No additional marker element is needed in the sparkline. Add a note in the mc-ref footer: "crossed threshold at [month]". This gives the customer a precise moment to point to in a GP conversation. Apply in both directions: entering range and leaving range, upper and lower bounds. A marker going above an upper reference limit uses the same convention — amber open circle on the first out-of-range point. See S7 Vitamin D for the entering-range reference implementation.

"Lower is better" inverted axis — hs-CRP (MVP scope only)

For markers where improvement means lower values, the sparkline y-axis is inverted: a falling line = positive progress. A LOWER BETTER label appears in the top-left corner of the sparkline SVG. The trend badge uses inverted logic: "↓ Improving" when the value falls, "↑ Worsening" when it rises. The reference threshold line still appears at the correct position. MVP scope: hs-CRP only (threshold <1.0 mg/L). Do not apply to Cortisol — it has an optimal midrange (140–700 nmol/L), not a lower-is-better relationship. If a future marker requires this treatment, the same CSS class handles it. See S7 hs-CRP for the reference implementation.
