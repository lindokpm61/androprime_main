The NHS "normal" testosterone range, roughly **8 to 29 nmol/L**, wasn't built to tell you whether you're functioning well. It was built to flag clinical illness. If your GP says your level is "within range" but you feel materially worse than you did five years ago, the range isn't lying. It just isn't answering your question.

## What "normal" actually means (without the textbook)

A man brought me his GP results recently. Total testosterone 12 nmol/L. "Within normal range." His GP had told him so.

I asked him one question.

What's the range?

8 to 29 nmol/L.

Most men assume that's a healthy band. It isn't. It's a statistical band: the spread you'd get if you took blood from every adult man between 18 and 70 and drew a line at the 2.5th and 97.5th percentile.

Athletes. Sedentary. Lean. Overweight. 22-year-olds. 68-year-olds. All in one pot.

The range was built to answer one question: is this man clinically ill?

It wasn't built to answer: is this man well?

**Those are two different questions.** The NHS test answers one of them. Most symptomatic men are asking the other.

<StatBox label="The statistical reality">
  The NHS range of 8 to 29 nmol/L spans roughly a 260% difference between the low end and the high end. Two men, one at 9 nmol/L, one at 25 nmol/L, both receive the same result: "within normal range."

  Clinically, they are not the same. The man at 9 nmol/L is statistically far more likely to report fatigue, low libido, and slow recovery. The man at 25 nmol/L is unlikely to report any of those. Both are categorised as normal.
</StatBox>

## Why the range is so wide

Three things make the reference range as wide as it is. None of them are about whether UK men are healthy.

### Age: the biggest single driver

Testosterone peaks in men in their early 20s. From the age of 30, it drops by roughly 1 to 2% per year.

By 45, the average man has lost 15 to 30% of his peak T.

By 60, more than 40%.

That decline is baked into the reference range. The sample includes 18-year-olds at peak and 70-year-olds in slow decline. When you draw a line at the 2.5th and 97.5th percentile across that whole pot, what you get is a span that accommodates physiology decades apart.

That's why the range is wide. It's not because UK men are healthy. It's because the range was built to fit men of all ages.

### The obesity shift across the UK population

Adipose tissue contains an enzyme called **aromatase**. Aromatase converts testosterone into estradiol. More body fat means more conversion. Lower testosterone is the result.

UK obesity prevalence has nearly tripled since 1980.

The reference range gets resampled over time. A pool sampled today contains a much higher proportion of men with metabolic syndrome and visceral adiposity than the pool sampled in 1990.

When the population shifts, the reference range shifts with it. **"Normal" today is lower than "normal" was thirty years ago.**

### Who else is in the sample

Sedentary office workers. Endurance athletes. Men with diabetes. Men with metabolic syndrome. Men on no medications. Men on five.

All in one pot.

The reference range tells you where most men sit. It doesn't tell you whether most men are well.

<PullQuote>
  Including older men with naturally declining testosterone in the same reference population as 22-year-olds mechanically lowers what the system calls "normal." The range itself is a moving target.
</PullQuote>

## The clinical threshold vs the optimal range

The NHS clinical threshold for testosterone deficiency, hypogonadism, sits at around **8 nmol/L**. Below that, most GPs will refer to endocrinology for further investigation. Above that, the system mostly leaves you alone.

Three bands matter.

### Below 8 nmol/L: the referral line

The NHS refers to endocrinology. Testosterone replacement therapy may be considered as part of a clinical conversation, through the NHS or a private specialist. Andro Prime doesn't sit in that conversation. We're a wellness platform. TRT decisions are clinical, and they belong with a clinician.

### 8 to 12 nmol/L: the grey zone

The guidance gets unclear. Some clinicians investigate further. Others note the result as "borderline normal" and suggest lifestyle changes.

Most men sitting at this level, who present with classic low-T symptoms, are told they're fine.

<Punchline>That's the population this article is for.</Punchline>

### Above 12 nmol/L: "fine" by the framework

The overwhelming majority of GPs will categorise this as normal and take no further action, regardless of symptoms.

If you're at 13 nmol/L with a Free Androgen Index in the lowest decile for your age, the NHS framework still calls it normal.

**The framework isn't lying. It's just not built to answer the question you're asking.**

<EvidenceBox citation="Source: Wu, F.C.W. et al. (2010). “Identification of Late-Onset Hypogonadism in Middle-Aged and Elderly Men.” New England Journal of Medicine 363: 123–135, EMAS cohort (3,369 men aged 40–79).">
  A 2010 study examined testosterone levels and symptom prevalence in **3,369 European men aged 40 to 79**.

  The probability of experiencing three or more classic hypogonadal symptoms (fatigue, low libido, erectile dysfunction) increased significantly at testosterone levels **below 11 nmol/L**.

  Eleven. Not eight.

  The NHS referral threshold sits 3 nmol/L below where symptoms become statistically likely. Men sitting between 8 and 11 nmol/L are far more likely to be symptomatic than men above 15, but under current NHS guidance, they don't qualify for treatment.
</EvidenceBox>

## What this means if your number came back "normal"

You've had the test. The result is "within range." Something still feels off.

Three possibilities.

You might genuinely be fine. Most men with normal results are. Symptoms can have other causes: sleep, body composition, training load, mood, life.

<Note>

If low energy or low mood is the symptom that actually brought you here, vitamin D is the other common, and far cheaper, thing to rule out first. [Our guide to low vitamin D symptoms](/blog/low-vitamin-d-symptoms) covers what to look for.

</Note>

You might be in the 8 to 12 grey zone with the symptom profile Wu's team found. The NHS framework calls you normal. The evidence base says symptoms become statistically likely below 11.

You might be above 12 nmol/L but in the lowest decile for your age cohort. The framework calls you normal. Your body might disagree.

The framework wasn't built to answer that. The blood test was, provided the test measures more than the headline number.

What changes when you have the full panel (Total T, SHBG, Free Androgen Index, Albumin, Free T) is the loop. Baseline. Adjust one variable: sleep, training, body comp, alcohol. Wait 8 to 12 weeks. Retest. See whether the number moves.

**A single reading is rarely actionable. A number you've watched move is.**

We don't currently offer TRT. We test, explain the number, and tell you the next step, including the GP-referral one when the result calls for it.

**See your GP within a few weeks if:**

- Your result sits **persistently between 8 and 11 nmol/L** across two tests, with three or more classic low-T symptoms.
- Significant body composition or metabolic shifts you haven't been able to explain.

**Go to your GP, not us, regardless of any reading, if:**

- You're under 30 and planning fertility: different conversation, different specialist.
- You have a known hypothalamic or pituitary condition.
