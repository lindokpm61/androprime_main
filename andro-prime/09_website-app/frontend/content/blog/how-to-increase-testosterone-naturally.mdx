The levers that genuinely move testosterone are the unglamorous ones: losing excess body fat, lifting weights, sleeping properly, and easing off alcohol and chronic stress. Not booster pills. None of it works overnight, and the smartest first step isn't to change everything at once. It's to find out where your level actually sits, so you know whether testosterone is even the thing to fix.

## What actually affects your testosterone (the honest short list)

A man in his forties told me he'd been "doing everything" to get his testosterone up. Cold showers. A booster off a podcast ad. A supplement stack that cost him more than his gym membership.

I had one thing I wanted to know before any of that.

What had he actually changed about his sleep, his weight, and his drinking?

He went quiet.

Because nothing on his list was on the real list. The things that move testosterone in a healthy man are boring, and they're free. Body fat. Strength training. Sleep. Alcohol. Stress.

That's roughly the order of impact for most men, and the rest is noise.

Here's the part the influencer content skips. Testosterone isn't a dial you turn with one trick. It's a downstream signal of how your body is doing overall. Carry too much fat, sleep badly, drink heavily, run yourself ragged, and the number tends to drift down. Sort those out, and for a lot of men it drifts back up on its own.

**It's not a willpower problem with a single hack. It's a whole-body input problem with a short list of levers.**

## How to increase testosterone naturally

So here's how to increase testosterone naturally, ranked by what the evidence actually supports, not by what sells supplements.

### Lose excess body fat (the biggest lever for most men)

If you're carrying extra weight, this is almost always the one that matters most.

Fat tissue contains an enzyme called aromatase, which converts testosterone into oestrogen. More excess fat means more conversion, which means less testosterone left over. It's a direct, physical link, not a vague wellness claim.

A 2013 review pooling 24 studies found that losing weight, by either a low-calorie diet or surgery, was followed by a meaningful rise in testosterone, and the more weight men lost, the bigger the rise (Corona et al., 2013, [Body weight loss reverts obesity-associated hypogonadotropic hypogonadism](https://academic.oup.com/ejendo/article-abstract/168/6/829/6659681)). The direction is consistent across the evidence base.

That doesn't mean a number guarantee, and it doesn't mean crash dieting. It means that for the man with a spare stone or two around the middle, getting that down is the single highest-yield thing he can do.

<Punchline>For most men carrying extra weight, the gym membership matters less than the waist measurement.</Punchline>

### Strength training and movement

Lifting weights is the exercise to increase testosterone people reach for first, and the picture is more honest than the gym-bro version.

A hard resistance session produces a short, temporary spike in testosterone afterwards. That much is real. What's less certain is whether training alone meaningfully lifts your resting, day-to-day level once you account for everything else. The research there is mixed.

So why train? Because strength work is one of the most reliable ways to lose fat, build muscle, sleep better and improve insulin sensitivity, and those are the things that move testosterone. Resistance training earns its place through the company it keeps, not through a magic post-workout surge.

Two or three sessions a week, compound movements, progressively heavier over time. That's the whole prescription. Less interesting than a supplement label. More effective.

### Sleep, the overlooked one

Most men chasing testosterone obsess over training and ignore the eight hours where their body actually makes it.

Testosterone is produced largely during sleep, and skimping on it shows up fast. In a tightly controlled study, ten healthy young men slept under five hours a night for one week. Their daytime testosterone fell by 10 to 15% (Leproult & Van Cauter, 2011, [Effect of 1 Week of Sleep Restriction on Testosterone Levels in Young Healthy Men](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21632481/), JAMA). One week. Healthy men. A double-digit drop.

That's the fastest honest lever on this list. Not because it's a hack, but because the damage from poor sleep arrives quickly, which means the recovery can too. Fixed wake time, a dark cool room, screens down before bed, and the late drink moved off the table.

If you sleep "enough" and still feel flat, consistency is usually the issue, not the hours on paper.

### Alcohol, stress, and the daily basics

These don't get headlines, and they quietly set the floor.

Heavy or regular drinking weighs on testosterone production, so the UK guide of staying under 14 units a week, spread out with drink-free days, isn't just a liver thing (NHS, [Alcohol units](https://www.nhs.uk/live-well/alcohol-advice/calculating-alcohol-units/)). Chronic stress keeps cortisol elevated, and cortisol and testosterone tend to pull in opposite directions: the man permanently switched on rarely has much left in the tank. Daylight, movement, and not running on four hours and a stress hormone do more than any capsule.

None of these is dramatic on its own. Stacked, they're most of the difference.

### Diet: what helps, what's oversold

Diet matters, mostly through one route: keeping you at a healthy body fat level. That's the lever, not any single hero food.

Eating enough protein, and not crash-dieting or cutting fat to nothing, matters too: very low-calorie and very-low-fat diets can push the wrong way. But the mechanism is still body composition and not running your body into the ground, not a magic nutrient. The flip side is the sea of "testosterone foods" lists online promising one egg or one root vegetable will transform your levels. They won't. We'll go deeper on diet in a dedicated guide; for now, treat the single-food claims with the scepticism they've earned.

## "How to increase testosterone levels quickly", the honest answer

This is the search a lot of men actually type, so here's the straight version on how to increase testosterone levels quickly.

There isn't a safe way to do it overnight.

The levers that work, body fat, training, sleep, alcohol, stress, move the needle over weeks to months of consistent change. The one exception is sleep, where a string of good nights can lift a sleep-suppressed level relatively fast, as that JAMA study implies in reverse. But "fast" still means days to weeks, not minutes, and it only recovers ground you lost.

Anything promising a quick, dramatic, drug-free spike is selling the gap between what you want and what's true. The honest framing: build the habits, give them eight to twelve weeks, then measure, so you can see what actually moved instead of guessing.

## What about boosters and supplements? (the hype check)

This is the section the booster industry hopes you skip. So let's be blunt.

Most over-the-counter "testosterone boosters" don't have good evidence behind the claim on the bottle. The ingredient blends change faster than anyone can study them, the studies that exist are often tiny or industry-run, and "supports healthy testosterone" on a label is marketing language, not a tested promise. A lot of these products lean on the hope you won't check.

There is one claim that stands on firmer, regulated ground. Zinc contributes to the maintenance of normal testosterone levels. Read that carefully: maintenance, not boosting. It helps keep a normal level normal, which matters if you're genuinely short on zinc. It is not a lever to push a healthy level higher, and no honest reading of it says otherwise.

So the sensible order isn't "buy a stack and hope". It's: fix the lifestyle levers, find out your actual level, and if there's a real gap, address it with your GP or a data-led choice rather than a hyped pill.

## When low testosterone is worth checking properly (the honest boundary)

Most men reading this are looking at lifestyle, and lifestyle is the right place to start. But some symptoms are a reason to get checked properly, not pushed through.

Persistent low sex drive, ongoing fatigue, low mood, loss of morning erections, or a noticeable drop in strength and recovery are the kinds of things clinical guidance flags as worth investigating, especially when several show up together (Hackett et al., 2023, [British Society for Sexual Medicine Guidelines on Male Adult Testosterone Deficiency](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10307648/), World Journal of Men's Health). The more of them you have, the more it's worth a proper look.

Read that the right way. Symptoms are a reason to get checked, not a self-diagnosis. None of them means "you have low testosterone". Plenty have other causes entirely, and if low energy is the thing you recognise most, that's often not a testosterone story at all: our guide to [why you might be tired all the time](/blog/why-am-i-always-tired) covers the everyday causes, which our Energy & Recovery Check (Kit 2) is built for.

## The smart first move: measure where you stand

So you're ready to change the inputs. Where do you start?

Not with a supplement, and not by changing ten things at once. Start by finding out where your testosterone actually sits.

Here's why that order matters. Without a baseline, you're guessing whether testosterone is even your problem, and you've got no way to tell whether your changes worked. Two men with the same symptoms can have completely different numbers, and the man who measures first knows which game he's playing. The one who doesn't is changing his whole life on a hunch.

A baseline turns "I think I feel a bit better" into "my number moved". You change one or two levers, hold them for eight to twelve weeks, and retest. That's the difference between guessing and knowing.
