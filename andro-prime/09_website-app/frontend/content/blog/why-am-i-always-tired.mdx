You're not lazy, and you're probably not ill. Most persistent tiredness in otherwise-healthy men traces to a short list of everyday inputs: broken sleep, a stress load that never switches off, training that's outrun recovery, alcohol, and blood-sugar swings. The fix usually starts there. Sometimes it doesn't, and this covers that too.

## What "tired all the time" actually is

A man told me recently he was tired all the time. Not sleepy. Flat. The kind where the coffee works for an hour and then the floor comes back up to meet you.

One question, then.

What's changed?

He thought about it. Nothing, really. Same job. Same training. Same sleep, roughly.

That "roughly" is where these things live.

There's a difference between being sleepy and being tired all the time. Sleepy is a signal you can fix tonight. The deeper flat feeling, the one that has you typing "why do I feel tired all the time" into your phone at 11pm, is rarely about one bad night. It's an accumulation. Small inputs, stacked, over weeks.

**It's not usually one big thing. It's five small things you stopped noticing.**

Here's the honest part. For most healthy men in their late 30s and 40s, the answer isn't dramatic. It's not a hidden disease. It's the boring stuff, added up. And the boring stuff is fixable.

## The lifestyle causes that explain most of it

The NHS lists the usual suspects behind everyday tiredness: not enough good sleep, stress, low mood, too little activity, alcohol, and caffeine (NHS, [Tiredness and fatigue](https://www.nhs.uk/conditions/tiredness-and-fatigue/)). None of them sound serious on their own. That's exactly why they get missed.

### Sleep you think is fine but isn't

Most men count hours. Hours aren't the number that matters.

You can spend eight hours in bed and get six of broken, shallow sleep. A nightcap, a scroll in the dark, a bedroom that's too warm, a wildly different weekend lie-in: each one chips at the quality while the hours on paper look fine. The NHS guide is simple: aim for 6 to 9 hours, and keep your sleep and wake times roughly the same (NHS, [Tiredness and fatigue](https://www.nhs.uk/conditions/tiredness-and-fatigue/)).

If you sleep "enough" and still wake up tired, consistency is usually the lever, not quantity.

### Stress and the wired-but-tired loop

Stress doesn't always feel like stress. Sometimes it just feels like being switched on at midnight when you want to be asleep.

A mind that won't power down keeps your body in a state that's poor at repairing you. You lie there tired but alert. You wake unrefreshed. The next day's low energy makes everything feel harder, which winds the spring tighter. That's the loop a lot of "why am I so tired all the time" searches are actually describing.

<Punchline>Wired but tired is still tired.</Punchline>

### Training load that's outrun recovery (or no training at all)

Two opposite problems land in the same place.

The man who trains hard, adds volume, skips the easy weeks, and never lets recovery catch up will feel flat and heavy, not strong. The man who's stopped moving almost entirely will feel just as drained, because the body that sits still all day stops being good at making energy on demand. Movement and recovery are a pair. Too much of one without the other, and you pay for it in the afternoon.

### Alcohol, caffeine timing, and blood-sugar swings

Three habits that quietly set the floor for how you feel.

Alcohol wrecks the back half of your night's sleep even when it helps you drop off. The UK guide is to stay under 14 units a week, spread across several days, with drink-free days in between (NHS, [Alcohol units](https://www.nhs.uk/live-well/alcohol-advice/calculating-alcohol-units/)). Caffeine after mid-afternoon lingers longer than most men assume. And a breakfast that's all fast carbs gives you a spike and then a slump that arrives, like clockwork, at 2pm.

None of these is dramatic. All of them are levers.

### The boring basics: water, daylight, sitting still

Mild dehydration reads as tiredness. A day with no daylight reads as a flat mood and a drifting body clock. Eight hours in a chair reads as heavy legs and a foggy head.

The boring basics are boring because they work.

## What to change this week

You don't need a protocol. Pick two or three of these and give them a fortnight.

- Set a fixed wake time, including weekends, and protect a 30-minute wind-down with the screens off.
- Move your last coffee to before 2pm.
- Trim the week's drinking and put two drink-free nights in.
- Add a daily walk, ideally in daylight, and an easy week into your training if you've been pushing.
- Build breakfast around protein and fibre, not just fast carbs.

That's it. The aim isn't a perfect month. It's enough of a change, held long enough, to see whether your energy moves.

<PullQuote>
  A single tired week tells you nothing. A fortnight of honest changes tells you whether the cause is on this list.
</PullQuote>

## When tiredness is more than lifestyle

Most of the time, this is lifestyle. Sometimes it isn't, and the honest move is to say so plainly.

<ClinicalInsight author="Dr Ewa Lindo" role="GMC-registered GP, Andro Prime medical reviewer">

What changes the picture is duration and company: tiredness that has lasted weeks despite real changes, or tiredness alongside other new symptoms, deserves to be looked at properly rather than pushed through."
</ClinicalInsight>

- It comes with unexplained weight loss, breathlessness, a low mood that won't lift, or any other new symptom that worries you.

**See your GP in the next few weeks if:**

- You've genuinely fixed the sleep, alcohol and training basics for a month and the tiredness hasn't moved.
- Feeling tired all the time is now your normal, not an off patch.

## Where a check actually fits

So you've changed the inputs. Two, three weeks in. Better, but not right. What now?

This is where a blood test earns its place, and not before. A check can show whether something measurable is contributing: vitamin D, Active B12 and iron are the common ones worth seeing. It isn't a diagnosis on its own, and it won't do the work the lifestyle basics do. What it gives you is a baseline, a few real numbers instead of guesses, and a clearer conversation if you do take it to your GP.

<Note>

If low mood and tiredness through the darker months is the thing you recognise most, low vitamin D is the cheap, common one to rule out first. [Our guide to low vitamin D symptoms](/blog/low-vitamin-d-symptoms) covers what to look for.

</Note>
