The Myth of the 'Normal' Range:
Why Reference Intervals Are Failing Men

Laboratory reference ranges for testosterone are derived from a cross-section of the population, not an optimised cohort. Here is why falling within the range doesn't mean you're functioning optimally.

When a patient receives their blood test results back from a standard clinic and sees their testosterone levels fall within the 'normal' range, there is usually a collective sigh of relief. The doctor signs off, and the patient assumes their persistent fatigue, difficulty maintaining lean mass, and lagging recovery are simply symptoms of getting older.

But what does 'normal' actually mean in the context of clinical diagnostics?

The Statistical Reality of Reference Ranges

Reference intervals (often called reference ranges) are established by testing a large cohort of individuals to determine what the typical values are for a population. Usually, the central 95% of the values are considered the reference range. This means that by definition, 2.5% of the population will fall below the range, and 2.5% will fall above it.

However, there is a fundamental flaw in this methodology when applied to hormones like testosterone: the population being sampled is becoming progressively less healthy.

"We are comparing individual patients to a population average that is experiencing unprecedented rates of obesity, metabolic syndrome, and endocrine disruption. 'Average' is no longer synonymous with 'healthy'."

Consider that the average testosterone levels in men have been declining by roughly 1% per year since the 1980s. A 50-year-old man today has significantly less testosterone than a 50-year-old man did 30 years ago. As the population average drops, the reference range is adjusted downwards to reflect the new 'normal'.

Total vs. Free: The Binding Dilemma

Furthermore, simply looking at Total Testosterone — the default marker used by most general practitioners — paints an incomplete picture.

  • Total Testosterone: The absolute amount of testosterone in the blood.
  • SHBG (Sex Hormone-Binding Globulin): A protein that binds to testosterone, rendering it inactive.
  • Free Testosterone: The unbound, bioavailable testosterone that tissues can actually use.

It is entirely possible (and quite common) to have a Total Testosterone level in the upper quartile of the normal range, but an elevated SHBG level that traps the majority of it. The result? Clinically deficient Free Testosterone, despite a 'normal' total reading.

Moving from Normal to Optimal

At Andro Prime, our clinical protocols do not aim for the statistical average. We aim for physiological optimisation. This requires a shift in paradigm from 'is there a disease present?' to 'is this system functioning at peak efficiency?'

True optimisation requires a comprehensive panel that looks at the entire male axis, not just a single snapshot of Total Testosterone. It requires evaluating the markers in the context of the individual's lifestyle, training load, and symptoms, rather than simply checking if a number falls between two arbitrary goalposts.
