What changed since v1

v1's spine was tested against a verified competitor teardown, an adversarial review, and a keyword backfill. Three of its own conclusions did not survive. This page is the rebuilt argument; the corrections are listed, not buried.

Refuted · 2026-07-20
“Every competitor nails one slice; none owns interpretation + longitudinal tracking + trust together. Owning that layer is the moat.”
v1 thesis, killed by the teardown: Thriva owns that exact triad (GP review, optimal-range trend dashboard, wearable sync, subscription). Forth largely does too. Medichecks has the best accreditation story on paper (CQC + UKAS ISO 15189 + GMC doctors). The “graveyard of failed apps” was indie free tools, not the funded commercial field. The “Results Brain” is table-stakes on contested ground, not a moat: still build good interpretation, stop calling it the strategy.

The defensible position

The market splits in two. We are the gap.

Men's-health testosterone brands are treatment funnels: their interpretation is structurally conflicted because they profit when your result is “low”. The diagnostics generalists are honest brokers, but they are not men's brands, so there is no low-T anxiety narrative for them to disarm. Nobody occupies a male-positioned testosterone brand that is deliberately not a treatment funnel.

Treatment funnels

Funnel

The low-T and ED click is their P&L. A “low” result is the conversion event.

  • NumanT test £58 (5 markers) → TRT ~£74.50/mo, ED meds
  • VoyT test £33.95 (from £54.99) → TRT from £99/mo, also GLP-1
  • ManualT test £33.95 → TRT from £59.99/mo, ED meds
  • Hims UKED meds and testosterone support, pure funnel

Andro Prime

Conflict-free

“The men's testosterone test with no reason to sell you testosterone.”

  • A GMC-registered GP reviews your result; a low result routes to your NHS GP, not a subscription.
  • UKAS-accredited UK lab; one UK data controller; our commitment: your data is never sold.
  • No TRT, no ED meds, no supplements pitch on a bad number. Nothing to upsell.
  • The funnels are commercially barred from this claim: the upsell is their revenue. The generalists don't make it: they aren't men's brands.
The constraint is the moat

Honest brokers

Generalist

Don't prescribe, genuinely credible, but testosterone is one panel among hundreds.

  • Thrivaowns the triad: GP review + trend dashboard + wearables. Subscription-gated, monetises GLP-1
  • Forthtracking + doctor review, from £40, male hormone kits. No prescribing
  • Medichecksbest accreditation on paper: CQC + UKAS ISO 15189 + GMC. One-off model
Honesty limits on this claim. Do not assert that rivals sell data (unverified); frame it as our positive commitment plus the structural observation that funnel businesses ingest low-T and ED intent for targeting. And “named GMC GP” is thin on its own (rivals also use GMC reviewers): lead on conflict-free and nothing-to-upsell, use the named GP as supporting texture.
Mainstream confirmation (2026-07-21). The deep-research pass (17 claims through 3-vote adversarial verification) confirmed the position lands beyond the forums: undisclosed paid follow-up tests and subscription traps are verified mainstream complaint clusters at Numan, including ASA ruling A22-1153049 (the “fear nothing” refund: only 4–10% qualified, subscription buried in the ad). The nuance that shapes copy: mainstream anger is billing distrust, not anti-TRT ideology. Mass-market copy leads with speed and no-GP (52% of private buyers buy “seen quicker”), then money honesty (“one price, nothing hidden, we earn the same either way”); the industry critique is the press/GEO layer. Reassurance is the category's centre of gravity: Medichecks refers only ~7% of customers to a GP and refuses subscriptions on clinical grounds. Revenue first · answers the “these are free tools” critique

What we sell, in order

B1 · Lead SKU

Prepaid two-kit bundles, now priced (working prices accepted 2026-07-21)

Every research pass named the retest-to-prove loop as the emotional payoff, then only proposed free reminders. Package it as paid two-kit SKUs instead, one honest shape per kit: Prove-It (Kit 2, flagship) £199 = £119 baseline + £80 day-90 retest (Vit D, B12, ferritin, hs-CRP genuinely move in 8–16 weeks); Confirmation (Kit 1) £169 = £99 baseline + £70 second morning sample only if low/borderline, banked as the 6–12-month recheck or refunded on an all-clear (testosterone does not move reliably in 90 days on wellness-legal inputs, so Kit 1's honest form is confirmation, not prove-it); Full-picture (Kit 3) £259 = £179 + the £80 Kit 2 retest. Pricing rule: the retest is ~30% off when prepaid, the first kit is never discounted, and the split is shown on the page. Ship one kit; dispatch the second on its trigger (day ~80, or the result). Pre-commits the buyer through the days 15–45 churn window, and manufactures the retest behaviour the category has never demonstrated (the one retention claim found in research died 0-3 in verification). Bundle contributions: £48 / £68 / £92.

Confidence: high · adversarial review B1 + pricing accepted 2026-07-21 · model: ltv-cac-profitability-model-2026-07-21.md
B2 · Gift SKU

“Give him the nudge”

Partner or family buys for the reluctant man (the tradie who won't lose a day's pay for the GP). The gifter is far less price-sensitive, so it side-steps the £33.95 funnel entry-price collision. Gift message, scheduled send, Father's Day and Christmas seasonality.

Confidence: medium
B3 · B2B / employer

SME wellness bulk kits

Per-seat or bulk kits sold to employers and occupational-health brokers. Lower CAC, higher volume, and the employer pays, which softens the D2C churn problem. Stays inside Phase 0 wellness. Validate with 3–5 broker conversations first.

Confidence: medium
B4 · Targeted panels

Ethnic and occupational risk SKUs

Black men 45+ (1-in-4 prostate cancer risk), South Asian men (cardiometabolic risk at lower BMI: ApoB, HbA1c, fasting insulin), and shift/HGV/offshore workers who can't take a GP day. Existing kits, targeted landing pages: higher intent, lower competition than generic heads.

Confidence: medium
B5 · Seasonal

Vitamin D “Winter Check” (Oct–Mar)

The whole UK sits above 50°N. “vitamin D test” is cheap, high-intent demand (2,400/mo, KD 8). A low-price seasonal front door that dodges the funnel price collision and feeds the wider range.

Confidence: medium
B6 · Interpretation SKU

Paid “Decode My Bloods”

A standalone low-ticket product for men who tested elsewhere: NHS or other-lab PDF in, a clinician-checked plain-English read out. Sells the reading, not the assay, so it has no £33.95 price floor. Ewa-gated wording throughout.

Confidence: medium

The keyword truth, caveats first

Read this before quoting any cluster total

The ten pains, and where each one now lands

Mined from ~90 forum threads across six domains; the same frustrations recurred everywhere. Directional signal, not sized data. What changed in v2 is not the pains but what we do with them.

01

GP gatekeeping and distrust

Dismissed, told “you're normal”, refused tests. The demand engine behind all private testing.

02

“Normal but I feel terrible”

A reference-range number that doesn't match symptoms, and nobody to explain the gap.

03

The interpretation gap

Numbers, then Google. Men post lab screenshots to strangers; DIY LLM reads misfire.

04

No longitudinal home for results

Static PDFs, no trends, no cross-lab merge. The loudest single feature request.

v2: table-stakes, not the moat. Thriva owns this.
05

Actionability

“What do I do now?” Universal; acute for cancer and genetic risk.

06

Choice paralysis and price opacity

Which test, which provider, and a 10–40x price spread on the same marker.

07

Finger-prick failure

Insufficient or haemolysed samples; the community's own fix is “get a venous draw”.

08

Anxiety-unaware delivery

A negative doesn't reassure; alarmist UI panics people over trivial results.

09

The retest-to-prove loop

Before/after is the emotional payoff; reversal posts are the highest-scoring content in their subs.

v2: became the lead SKU (B1).
10

Anti-upsell allergy and privacy

Users punish thin upsell wrappers and data resale. Trust is the battleground, not breadth.

v2: became the headline position. v2.1: confirmed mainstream (Numan Trustpilot + ASA ruling), expressed as billing distrust: lead with money honesty.

Copy rails · every external word

Constraints, restated

  • Phase 0 is wellness only. No TRT, no clinical claims, no prescribing. Clinical mode waits for CQC.
  • A low result routes to the customer's GP. Never to a product. This is the moat; never soften it into an upsell.
  • Never write “we will never sell testosterone.” The post-CQC clinic plans TRT; the absolute version detonates on launch day. The durable claim is separation of incentives: testing and treatment are separated, we earn the same whether your result is low or fine, and treatment only ever follows an independent clinical decision.
  • The lab is named only as “a UKAS-accredited UK lab” in anything customer-facing.
  • The silent-ingredient rule holds everywhere, including this page and everything derived from it.
  • Never assert that competitors sell data. Unverified. State our commitment; let the structural contrast speak.
  • No em dashes in customer-facing copy. Colons, commas, semicolons, brackets.
  • PSA and “Decode My Bloods” wording is Ewa-gated before anything ships.
