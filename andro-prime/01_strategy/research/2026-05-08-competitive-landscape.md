## Executive summary

- **Kit 1 sits in the most crowded, most price-compressed category in the UK market.** Direct testosterone-only competitors range from MyHealthChecked at £29.99 (Boots own-label) and Manual/Voy/Optimale at £33.95, through Vitall (the existing lab partner) at ~£79, to Medichecks' base T tests broadly £29–£49. At £99, Kit 1 is roughly **2.5–3.4x the cheapest comparable home T test**, and **~25% above LetsGetChecked's basic Testosterone Test.** (Confidence: M.)
- **Kit 2 sits in genuine whitespace.** No mainstream UK at-home brand offers a tightly-scoped "Energy & Recovery" 4-marker panel (Vit D, Active B12, hs-CRP, Ferritin) at a comparable price point. Closest analogues are Forth's sports panels and Thriva's energy add-ons. **Kit 2 at £119 is the most defensible price in the range.** (Confidence: M.)
- **Kit 3 at £179 is competitively priced — but on the wrong axis.** Randox Everyman is £295 with 150 data points; Medichecks Advanced Well Man is ~£159 with 44 markers; LetsGetChecked Male Hormone Advanced is £149 with 5 hormone markers. Kit 3 wins on interpretation quality, not breadth. The existing decision in `icp-kit-supplement-alignment-april2026.md` to drop the "MOT" framing is **strategically correct and supported by this data**. (Confidence: H — internal doc + external pricing confirms.)
- **Kit 1 vs Medichecks £35 IS a real comparison-shopping risk, but not a kill-shot.** Trustpilot signal for cheap testosterone tests shows recurring complaints about result interpretation ("doctor's comments not very helpful," "lack of real help with… whether I needed medical intervention"). The £99 GP-interpreted wrapper is genuinely defensible — but only if the interpretation is visibly better than Medichecks', and the price gap is justified in copy. The risk is bigger from MyHealthChecked at £29.99 (Boots distribution, mass-market trust) than from Medichecks. (Confidence: M.)
- **Result-mapped supplement subscriptions are a US pattern that has NOT been productised in the UK at-home men's health space.** Hone Health (US, $129–149/mo) does it. Function Health (US, $365/yr) does it. InsideTracker (US, $149/yr + tests) does it. **In the UK, the closest analogues are Vitl and Bioniq PRO — but neither owns the test-to-supplement bridge end-to-end for the men's health ICP.** This is genuine whitespace. Option 4 has no UK precedent to copy and no UK precedent to compete against. (Confidence: H.)
- **Numan is the dominant scale player by a wide margin.** $113M raised, $90M revenue (2024), 650k+ patients treated, 33,000+ Trustpilot reviews. Their £33.95 finger-prick → £74.50 venous → TRT funnel is the architectural playbook Andro Prime is implicitly competing with. They DO offer GP consultation included in the test price. Voy (the rebranded Manual) operates the same playbook. (Confidence: H.)
- **The brutal observation:** Numan and Voy both already run a £33.95 test → TRT subscription funnel with GP consultation. Andro Prime's Kit 1 at £99 with low-T → founding-member-deferred-TRT pathway is **the same architecture at 2.9x the price, with a longer wait, and pre-CQC.** Without Kit 2 cashflow and without a result-mapped supplement bridge, Kit 1 has a hard time competing on its own.
- **The strongest argument for Option 4 (Kit 1 + supplement, decouple founding member):** the brands that have scaled in this space all have a recurring-revenue product attached to the test. Numan has TRT, weight-loss meds, ED meds. Voy has the same. Forth has subscription kits. Thriva has subscription tests. **No mainstream UK player is selling a result-driven supplement subscription bridged from a testosterone test.** That is the gap Option 4 would occupy.

## Kit positioning analysis

### Kit 1 (£99, Testosterone Health Check) — testosterone-only

**Cheaper than Kit 1:**
- MyHealthChecked at Boots — £29.99 (3.3x cheaper, mass distribution, full T panel including SHBG/FAI/free T)
- Manual/Voy — £33.95 (2.9x cheaper, finger-prick, GP-led, route into TRT subscription)
- Optimale — £33.95 (same)
- Numan finger-prick — £33.95 (same; 2.9x cheaper, GP consult included)
- Forth — from £40 (2.5x cheaper)
- Medichecks base testosterone test — from ~£39 (2.5x cheaper)

**Equivalent to Kit 1:**
- Vitall — ~£79 (slightly cheaper; Andro Prime's own lab partner)

**More expensive than Kit 1:**
- LetsGetChecked Male Hormone Advanced — £149 (5 hormone markers, broader panel)
- Numan venous — £149 (£74.50 promoted)
- Medichecks Male Hormone Panel — ~£89+ (broader panel — actually equivalent or slightly cheaper)

**What you get more for the £99:**
- (Per the existing positioning) GP-led plain-English interpretation + Keith's voice + the wellness mode → founding-member pathway. The interpretation depth and UX is the only credible differentiator — the panel isn't broader than Numan or Manual at £33.95.

**What you get less for the £99:**
- No GP-prescribed treatment pathway (Numan, Voy, Optimale all bundle TRT). Andro Prime can't because it's pre-CQC.
- No high-street trust signal (Boots/Lloyds). Andro Prime is direct-to-consumer only.

**Price-defensibility score: LOW.**

Justification: Andro Prime is selling at 2.5–3.3x the cheapest direct competitor with the same finger-prick + testosterone panel + GP interpretation, with no prescribing pathway, no high-street brand trust, no scale-signal Trustpilot count, and no direct subscription bridge. The £99 price is defensible only if the GP-interpreted plain-English wrapper feels visibly better than Numan's £33.95 and Boots' £29.99 — and that's a perception fight, not a structural advantage.

### Kit 2 (£119, Energy & Recovery Check)

**Cheaper than Kit 2:**
- Thriva subscription tests with Vit D / B12 / energy add-ons — typically £30–60 per test
- Forth Baseline subscription — £59/kit
- Medichecks individual Vit D, B12, Ferritin, hs-CRP tests — ~£29–£39 each (but customer would need to buy 4 separate tests = £116–£156 total)

**Equivalent to Kit 2:**
- Forth sports/recovery panels — £89–£99 typically
- Forth Build-Your-Own with 4 markers — likely £80–£120

**More expensive than Kit 2:**
- Bluecrest Wellness annual packages from £179
- Randox Everyman £295

**Whitespace observation:** No mainstream UK at-home brand has packaged exactly these 4 markers (Vit D, Active B12, hs-CRP, Ferritin) into a single "Energy & Recovery" SKU at £119 with GP-led interpretation and a result-mapped supplement subscription. Forth comes closest but doesn't bridge to a supplement.

**Price-defensibility score: HIGH.**

Justification: The 4-marker bundle matches actual ICP 2 symptom logic, the price is in line with Forth's sports panels but with a sharper result-to-supplement bridge (which Forth doesn't have), and the buy-individual-markers alternative at Medichecks costs roughly the same. The £119 is the right price; the moat is the bundle + interpretation + supplement bridge, not raw markers.

### Kit 3 (£179, Hormone & Recovery Check)

**Cheaper than Kit 3:**
- Medichecks Advanced Well Man — £159 (44 biomarkers, much broader)
- LetsGetChecked Male Hormone Advanced — £149 (5 hormone markers, narrower than Kit 3)
- Bluecrest Wellness annual health check — from £179 (clinic-based, comparable at the price)
- Forth Advanced Health Check — likely £149–£199

**Equivalent to Kit 3:**
- Bluecrest packages from £179

**More expensive than Kit 3:**
- Randox Everyman — £295 (150 data points; in-clinic; 6-month retest included; massively broader)

**The expectation gap (already flagged in icp-kit-supplement-alignment-april2026.md, confirmed by this data):** A buyer comparing Kit 3 (£179, 9 markers) to Medichecks Advanced Well Man (£159, 44 markers) sees a 4.9x marker-density gap at a 12.6% price premium. The MOT framing makes this gap dangerous. The "9 targeted markers, GP-interpreted, plain-English" framing is defensible.

**Price-defensibility score: MEDIUM.**

Justification: Kit 3 sits in genuinely competitive territory with Bluecrest and Forth at the same price. It is undercut by Medichecks on raw marker breadth. It wins on interpretation quality and ICP fit. The decision in `icp-kit-supplement-alignment-april2026.md` to drop "MOT" framing is essential — without that change the price-defensibility drops to Low.

## Implications by option

### Option 1 — Demote Kit 1 (lead with Kit 2 + Kit 3)

- **Pricing data supports this.** Kit 1's price-defensibility is Low against Numan/Voy/Optimale at £33.95 and MyHealthChecked at £29.99. Demoting it removes the comparison-shopping pressure point.
- **Risk:** ICP 1 traffic from the symptom-recognition LinkedIn/founder-content engine has nowhere to land in Phase 0 except a deferred founding-member promise. That's a long, lossy funnel.
- **Phase 0 cashflow:** Kit 2 at £119 + supplement subscription is the highest-value path, this option doubles down on it.
- **Brand consequence:** Kit 1 demotion implicitly accepts that Andro Prime can't compete with Numan/Voy at the £33.95 acquisition layer pre-CQC. Honest, but cedes ICP 1 acquisition.

### Option 2 — Reposition Kit 1 (add 1–2 recovery markers)

- **Pricing data partially supports this.** A 4-marker testosterone-plus-recovery test at £99 is genuinely differentiated from Numan's £33.95 T-only finger-prick.
- **But:** It bumps Kit 1 into Forth's sports panels territory (£89–£99 with comparable marker breadth) where Forth has scale, app, retesting, and Trustpilot 4.5 / 1,600+ reviews. The defensibility moves from "low (vs Medichecks)" to "medium (vs Forth)."
- **Risk:** Cannibalises Kit 2. If Kit 1 has Vit D + B12 added, the line between Kit 1 and Kit 2 blurs. The decision tree gets harder. (Existing `icp-kit-supplement-alignment-april2026.md` is explicit about *not* blurring this.)
- **Verdict:** Tactically appealing, strategically risky. The kit architecture is currently clean — this option introduces overlap.

### Option 3 — Drop Kit 1 from Phase 0 entirely

- **Pricing data supports the structural argument.** Kit 1 cannot win on price, marker breadth, or treatment pathway against Numan/Voy/Optimale pre-CQC. It can only win on interpretation quality and brand voice.
- **Risk:** Eliminates the founding-member acquisition funnel entirely until Kit 3 traffic is meaningful. Kit 3 is an ICP 3 product (curious maintainer) — these are not the same buyers as ICP 1. The founding-member pipeline becomes ICP-mismatched.
- **Verdict:** Cleanest commercially, weakest for long-term TRT pipeline build. If post-CQC TRT subscription is the strategic prize, this option starves the pipeline.

### Option 4 — Pair Kit 1 with supplement subscription regardless of result; decouple founding-member

**This is the option the competitive data supports most strongly.** Reasons:

- **No UK precedent = whitespace.** Bioniq PRO is closest but doesn't own the test. humanpeople is closest but isn't men's-health-positioned. Andro Prime can be the first UK brand to vertically integrate test → result-mapped supplement subscription for men's health.
- **Hone Health validates the architecture in the US.** Same model, different regulatory layer. Hone has 5+ years and 600+ reviews proving the pattern monetises.
- **Solves the £99 vs £30 comparison-shopping problem structurally, not by discounting.** Kit 1 is no longer "a £99 testosterone test" — it's "the entry point to a result-driven supplement subscription, with founding-member access for those who need it." That reframes the buyer's decision: they're not comparing £99 to MyHealthChecked's £29.99; they're comparing an LTV-bearing relationship to a one-off result.
- **Decoupling founding member from low T is consistent with the v2.2 marketing plan and the wellness/clinical split.** The current model creates a perverse dynamic where the cheapest customer (low T → founding member deferred) is the highest-LTV one, and the supplement-subscription-eligible (normal T) is the dead-end. Option 4 fixes that.
