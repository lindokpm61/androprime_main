## Post 1 — The instrumentation problem

A founder with twelve staff told me the problem was trust.

"Last time someone priced a job without me, they agreed to terms that lost us money. So now I check everything."

Every invoice. Every proposal. Every client quote.

I asked one question: where's the document that tells your team how to price a job?

The margins. The discount rules. The minimum project value. The walk-away number.

Silence.

There wasn't one.

The entire commercial logic of the business lived in one place. The founder's head. Twelve people, growing fast, and every commercial decision funnelled through a single brain because nobody had ever written the rules down.

I've been talking this week about what happens when founders can't stop checking their team's work. Here's what's underneath it.

The staff weren't incompetent. They were guessing. They had no framework to follow, no decision criteria, no authority boundaries. Every time they got it wrong, it confirmed the founder's belief that they couldn't be trusted. So the checking intensified. And the team learned to wait rather than decide.

This is what scaling looks like when you grow the headcount but not the infrastructure. The business outgrew the founder's ability to hold everything informally. But the formal systems that should have replaced that awareness were never built.

It's not a trust problem. It's an instrumentation problem. You're trying to run a 15-person business using a 5-person nervous system.

The fix isn't more communication. It's codifying what's in your head so your team can use it without you.

## Post 2 — He didn't have a client problem

He was signing off every out-of-scope request on a £60K-a-year account and calling it client management.

Every week, something outside the original quote arrived like a standard request. Invoices 30, 40 days late every cycle. His team said this 1 client was taking more time than the next 3 combined. And every escalation, straight to him.

He wanted me to tell him the client was the problem.

I asked him 1 question first.

"What happened the last time a client asked for something outside the quote?"

He paused. Told me about a job that ran 3 weeks over because the brief kept moving. The client kept adding. He kept absorbing. No document to point at. No process for what happened when scope shifted.

I asked him to show me his payment terms and the escalation path if a client breached them.

He pulled up a PDF. Standard 30 days. No late fee. No follow-up process documented anywhere.

"What actually happens when they don't pay on time?"
"We chase them."

"And if they keep not paying?"

"We chase them again."

2 questions. The whole thing was visible.

He didn't have a client problem.

He had no rules for how clients were supposed to behave. Nothing written down for scope, payment, or escalation. The client didn't exploit a gap. They walked through an open door.

3 things needed to exist. A change request process with a price attached to deviation from scope. Payment terms with a late fee that gets applied. An escalation path that ends with the team for anything routine.

He built all 3. Took about a week.

The client is still on the books. Last invoice paid on time.

What happens in your business when a client asks for something that wasn't in the quote?

## Post 3 — The untracked liability

Your best closer isn't your biggest asset. He's your most expensive untracked liability.

Here's what I mean.

I spoke with a business owner recently who was going back and forth about whether to fire his top sales rep. Closed more than anyone. Pipeline always full. But clients on his book churned at double the rate of every other rep's accounts.

When I asked how much each churned client was actually costing the business, he stopped.

He'd never calculated it. Not once.

The rep had a line for it: "my job is to close, delivery's job to deliver." Which would be fine if the two were genuinely separate. They're not. Every promise made in the pitch is a debt the delivery team inherits. Every timeline implied, every deliverable implied, every version of the product that exists in the proposal but not in the building — that's a debt with a three-month repayment window.

Two other reps had started copying the approach. Because they could see the commission cheques. Nobody had shown them where the money was actually leaving.

The owner didn't have a rep problem. He had a measurement problem. The incentive structure rewarded signatures and said nothing about what happened after them. So the person creating the most downstream cost looked, on every metric being tracked, like the best person in the room.

You don't fix that by firing someone. You fix it by changing what you pay for.

Retention at three months. Margin at six. Something that makes the cost of a churned client land on the same spreadsheet as the commission that created it.

What's the most expensive decision your current commission structure is quietly rewarding?

## Post 4 — One team commissioned it, another claimed it

One team commissioned it. Another team claimed it.

During testing, the finance team saw what the logistics team had built and had an idea.

20-person business. 6-month software build. The logistics team had a widget pulling from their own data. Built exactly to brief. Working. Finance weren't part of the project. Hadn't been in any of the conversations. But they were in the building when testing started, they saw what it did, and they thought: if it does that for logistics, it could do the same for us.

Reasonable assumption. Wrong conclusion.

I asked him one question.

"Who else in the business might need something from this when it goes live?"

Nobody had asked that in the sales process. The brief was scoped with the logistics team because the logistics team had the problem. Finance didn't know the project existed until they walked into a testing session.

The request looked like an extension. It was a new integration. Finance's data had never been part of the conversation. Getting their version to work meant starting again with a data set nobody had touched. That was outside the original scope. By some distance.

3 weeks of back and forth followed. Finance couldn't fund it from their budget. Logistics weren't prepared to cover it from theirs. Finance went away without what they wanted. Logistics went live with exactly what they'd originally signed off.

He didn't have a scope creep problem.

Nobody had asked which departments would need something from this before the brief was written. The logistics team bought something. The finance team inherited it the moment it went live.

One question gets added to every discovery call now. Who else in the business is going to need something from this when it's done.

That question costs nothing to ask. Finding out during testing costs considerably more.

When did you last scope a project with every team that would touch the output, not just the one that signed the contract?
