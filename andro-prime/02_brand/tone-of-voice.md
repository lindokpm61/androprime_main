# Andro Prime — Tone of Voice

This document defines what "the Andro Prime voice" is and how to apply it. The voice is Keith Antony's. Everything customer-facing inherits from it.

It exists because a previous Andro Prime article (`the-myth-of-the-normal-range.mdx`) was published in a clinical-academic register that read nothing like Keith. The cause was simple: no canonical voice spec for anyone — agent, contractor, or future Claude session — to read before drafting. This is that spec.

This is v1. Calibrate further as more samples are added.

---

## 1. The voice in one line

**A diagnostic observer who tells stories about real moments, asks one question, and reframes the problem.**

Not a clinician. Not a wellness coach. Not a corporate brand. A man who has been through this problem himself, has watched many other men go through it, and writes like he's explaining what he's actually seen.

---

## 2. The five structural moves

Every well-formed Keith paragraph or arc does some combination of these. The samples in `references/voice-samples/` show them stacked.

### Move 1 — Open with a concrete moment, not a claim

Lead with a person, a number, a quote, or a specific occasion. Never an abstract opener.

- ✅ "A founder with twelve staff told me the problem was trust."
- ✅ "He was signing off every out-of-scope request on a £60K-a-year account and calling it client management."
- ✅ "I spent two years being told I was normal."
- ❌ "The reference range used by most UK laboratories for testosterone in adult men is approximately 8.64–29 nmol/L."
- ❌ "Inflammation is a complex biological process."

### Move 2 — Ask one question

"I asked one question" / "I asked him one question first" / "I asked him to show me…". Used in every long-form sample. The question itself is short, plain, and pointed.

- ✅ "Where's the document that tells your team how to price a job?"
- ✅ "What happened the last time a client asked for something outside the quote?"
- ✅ "Who else in the business might need something from this when it goes live?"

In a health context this looks like: "What did the GP actually test?" / "How long has this been going on?" / "What number did they give you?"

### Move 3 — Silence / realisation

The diagnostic question hits a gap. Mark the gap in plain language. Often one-word paragraphs.

- "Silence."
- "There wasn't one."
- "He paused."
- "Nobody had asked that in the sales process."

### Move 4 — The reframe pivot ("It's not X. It's Y.")

The signature move. Present in 4 of 5 samples. State the surface problem, then name the underlying one.

- ✅ "It's not a trust problem. It's an instrumentation problem."
- ✅ "He didn't have a client problem. He had no rules for how clients were supposed to behave."
- ✅ "He didn't have a rep problem. He had a measurement problem."
- ✅ "He didn't have a scope creep problem. Nobody had asked which departments would need something from this before the brief was written."

In a health context: "You don't have a willpower problem. You have a data problem." / "He didn't have a testosterone problem. He had a question-mismatch problem."

### Move 5 — Close with a question to the reader

Never a directive. Never "you should". Open a door back to the reader's own situation.

- ✅ "What happens in your business when a client asks for something that wasn't in the quote?"
- ✅ "What's the most expensive decision your current commission structure is quietly rewarding?"
- ✅ "When did you last scope a project with every team that would touch the output, not just the one that signed the contract?"

In a health context: "What was the last test your GP actually ordered, and what did the number mean?" / "What would you do differently if you knew where you actually sat on the scale?"

## 3. Sentence-level rules

### Fragments are deliberate

Short. Fragments. One-word paragraphs. Use them at pivot moments — never as filler.

```text
Silence.
There wasn't one.
Reasonable assumption. Wrong conclusion.
Took about a week.
```

Long-flow prose without fragments reads as off-voice. Every 150–200 words should have at least one fragment break.

### Triadic rhythm — rule of three

When listing, three items, all fragments, no list punctuation, separated by periods. Pattern locked across samples.

- "Every invoice. Every proposal. Every client quote."
- "The margins. The discount rules. The walk-away number."
- "No framework. No criteria. No authority boundaries."

Health version: "Total T. SHBG. Free T." / "The session you'd shake off in a day. The 2pm slump. The recovery that won't close."

### Contractions always

`isn't`, `doesn't`, `can't`, `won't`, `here's`, `it's`, `you're`, `we've`. Full forms read as off-voice. Rare exception: where the full form lands a particular rhetorical beat (e.g. "I will not" for emphasis).

### No em dashes

Never use the em dash (`—`) in customer-facing copy. It is the single clearest "written by AI" tell, and it is banned across all articles, landing pages, emails, ads, and social posts. Replace each one with the punctuation the grammar actually calls for:

- **Parenthetical aside** (`X — aside — Y`): use commas, or brackets if it's a true aside. "None of them, on their own or in clusters, replaces a blood test."
- **Lead-in to a list or elaboration** (`X — a, b, c`): use a colon. "Tiredness has many causes: sleep quality, iron, thyroid, training load."
- **Two independent clauses**: use a semicolon, or split into two sentences. "A symptom list can't tell you that; a blood test can."
- **Appositive / restatement** (`X — that's the thing`): recast with a verb or a colon. "Under 25 nmol/L is NICE-defined deficient."

En dashes and hyphens in numeric ranges are fine (or just write "X to Y"). The ban is specifically the em dash used as a sentence-level pause.

### Concrete numbers, never abstract qualifiers

| Banned qualifier | Use instead |
| --- | --- |
| significantly / substantially | the actual number, percentage, or duration |
| various / multiple / several | three / four / the specific list |
| considerable / extensive | the size in pounds, hours, headcount |
| approximately | the specific number, OR "around X" / "about X" |
| in many cases | in seven of ten cases / 70% / most |
| widely / commonly | named percentages or comparators |

- ❌ "Testosterone levels in men decline significantly with age."
- ✅ "Testosterone drops about 1–2% per year from age 30."

### Sentence length distribution

Aim for: ~30% short (5–10 words), ~50% medium (11–20 words), ~20% long (21–35 words). Almost never above 35. The existing myth article runs many sentences past 40 words — that is the principal density problem.

Rhythm rule: a long sentence should be followed by a short one. Never two long sentences back-to-back.

### Paragraph length

LinkedIn posts: 1–3 sentences per paragraph, with single-sentence paragraphs at pivot moments.

Blog: 2–5 sentences per paragraph, with fragment paragraphs deliberately placed every ~150 words for rhythm.

Never write a paragraph that fills the screen. If it does, break it.

## 4. Vocabulary discipline

### Use plain English — define any technical term in the same sentence it appears

If you can't define it without breaking flow, the term is being introduced too early — restructure so it earns its place.

- ❌ "hs-CRP is a sensitive inflammatory marker reflecting hepatic acute-phase reactant synthesis."
- ✅ "hs-CRP stands for high-sensitivity C-reactive protein. It's a protein your liver releases when your immune system is irritating it."

### Words you don't use (voice level, not compliance level)

These are voice off-notes — they push the writing into corporate or clinical register. Compliance has its own banned list at `/03_compliance/CONTEXT.md` Section 5; this list is additional.

- "Leverage" (as a verb) — use "use"
- "Stakeholders" — name them: "your GP", "your partner", "your coach"
- "Synergies" / "synergistic" — just describe what happens
- "Optimise" — banned in supplement copy by compliance anyway; in body copy use "improve" or be more specific
- "Empower" / "empowerment" — describe the action, not the feeling
- "Holistic" — describe what you actually mean
- "Journey" (as in "your wellness journey") — describe the actual sequence
- "Solution" — name the thing
- "Best-in-class" / "world-class" — describe what it actually is (UKAS-accredited, GMC-registered, etc.)
- "Robust" — describe the specific quality
- "Comprehensive" — list the actual contents
- "Cutting-edge" / "innovative" — describe what's new about it
- "Inherently" / "fundamentally" — almost always padding
- "It is important to note that…" — just say the thing

### Words you do use

Direct, concrete, common. Where there's a Latin/Greek polysyllabic and an Anglo-Saxon plain word for the same idea, the plain word almost always wins.

- "show" not "demonstrate"
- "use" not "utilise"
- "ask" not "enquire"
- "find out" not "ascertain"
- "next step" not "next action item"

### UK English, always

`colour`, `centre`, `recognise`, `optimise` (in spelling), `personalised`, `behaviour`, `programme`, `practise` (verb) / `practice` (noun). Never US spellings. Numbers in body copy: spell out one through nine in flowing prose; use digits for 10+ and for any measurement, money, time, or percentage.

## 5. Voice posture

### Diagnostic, not prescriptive

You watch, you ask, you name the underlying problem. You don't lecture. The reader's realisation is the thing — not your delivery of insight.

Avoid:

- "You need to…"
- "You should…"
- "What you have to understand is…"
- "Let me tell you why…"

Prefer:

- "Most men don't realise…"
- "Here's what happens when…"
- "Two things tend to be true at this point…"

### First person when you step in, third person when you observe

When telling someone else's story (the founder, the client, the GP visit), third person carries it. When you intervene with the diagnostic question, switch to first.

- "He was signing off every out-of-scope request… **I asked him one question first.** *What happened the last time a client asked for something outside the quote?* He paused."

When telling your own story (the bio, the About page), first person throughout.

### Insight is framed as the subject's realisation, not your delivery

- ❌ "I told him the issue was instrumentation."
- ✅ "Two questions. The whole thing was visible."

The reader credits the founder for the realisation. You're just the one who asked the question that made it visible.

### Never preachy, never moralising

Health content is a YMYL category — people are vulnerable when they read it. Tone that reads as smug or judgmental kills trust faster than anything else. The reader is not stupid for trusting their GP. The standard test was not designed for the question they were asking. That distinction matters.

## 7. Health content translation

Keith's natural subject in the canonical samples is business advisory. Andro Prime's subject is men's health diagnostics. The voice patterns port directly. The translation rules:

### Lead with the body-feel, not the marker

The man does not search for "hs-CRP". He searches for "why does recovery take three days now when it used to take one." Write to the feeling first, define the marker after.

- ✅ "The session you'd shake off in a day now takes three. The post-gym ache lingers. Sleep is the same, diet is roughly the same — but the body isn't. That's what an inflammatory markers blood test picks up."
- ❌ "hs-CRP is a sensitive marker of low-grade systemic inflammation correlated with metabolic and cardiovascular risk."

### Use the diagnostic question device for the topic, not for selling

The question is "what did the GP actually test?" or "what number did they give you?" — not "what's your wellness goal?". Sales framing kills trust.

### Adopt compliance hedging without losing the voice

The compliance phrases ("your results may show", "we don't diagnose", "speak to your GP if…") are not in tension with this voice. They land naturally when they're part of the diagnostic-observer posture.

- ✅ "Your results may show your hs-CRP is elevated. That tells you there's a signal. It doesn't tell you what's causing it. That's a conversation with your GP, not a supplement order."

### Name the boundary with the NHS / GP, don't apologise for it

Phase 0 boundary is a feature, not a limitation. The reader respects you more for being clear about what you don't do.

- ✅ "We don't diagnose. We don't prescribe. We test, explain the number, and tell you the next step — including when that next step is your GP."
- ❌ "While we are unable to provide a clinical diagnosis, we offer detailed reporting…"

## 8. Side-by-side — voice ON vs voice OFF

Pulled from the actual existing myth-of-normal-range article (voice OFF) and rewritten to the spec (voice ON). Use this as a calibration reference when reviewing any new draft.

### Same idea, two versions

**Voice OFF (existing article):**

> The reference range used by most UK laboratories for testosterone in adult men is approximately 8.64–29 nmol/L. This range was not defined by identifying the levels at which men feel and perform optimally. It was established through a statistical methodology called percentile-based sampling. In practice, this means the laboratory took blood samples from a broad cross-section of male participants (ranging in age from 18 to 70+, varying significantly in fitness, body composition, metabolic health, and lifestyle) and applied a 2.5th to 97.5th percentile distribution to define the "normal" range. The purpose of this methodology is to capture the range within which "most people" fall. It is explicitly designed to identify clinical outliers, not to assess quality of function.

(190 words. 4 long-flow sentences. No fragments. No reframe pivot. Third-person passive. No reader entry point.)

**Voice ON (rewrite):**

> A man brought me his GP results last month. Total testosterone 12 nmol/L. "Within normal range." His GP had told him so.
>
> I asked him one question.
>
> What's the range?
>
> 8 to 29 nmol/L.
>
> Most men assume that's a healthy band. It isn't. It's a statistical band — the spread you'd get if you took blood from every adult man between 18 and 70 and drew a line at the 2.5th and 97.5th percentile.
>
> Athletes. Sedentary. Lean. Overweight. 22-year-olds. 68-year-olds. All in one pot.
>
> The range was never designed to answer "are you well." It was designed to answer "are you ill." Two different questions. The NHS test answers one of them. Most men ask the other.
>
> He didn't have a normal-range problem. He had a question-mismatch problem.

(140 words. Mixed lengths. Three fragment paragraphs. The "I asked one question" device. Triadic rhythm. The signature reframe at the end. Reader enters through the man bringing his results — a moment, not an abstraction.)

Both pieces carry the same information. The second is in voice. The first is not.

## 9. Self-check checklist (apply to every new draft before review)

If a draft fails three or more of these, it's not in voice. Send back for revision.

- [ ] Opens with a concrete moment / number / quote — not an abstract claim
- [ ] Contains at least one "I asked X" or equivalent diagnostic question device per major section
- [ ] Contains at least one "It's not X. It's Y." reframe (or equivalent)
- [ ] Contains at least one triadic-rhythm passage (three items, fragments, periods)
- [ ] Sentence-length distribution: no two long sentences back-to-back
- [ ] At least one fragment paragraph per ~150 words
- [ ] Closes with a question to the reader, not a directive
- [ ] Contractions used throughout
- [ ] No em dashes (`—`) anywhere; each replaced with a colon, comma, semicolon, period, or brackets as the grammar requires
- [ ] No banned voice-off words (significantly, various, leverage, stakeholders, robust, etc. — full list Section 4)
- [ ] No "you should…" anywhere
- [ ] Reading level ≤ UK Year 10 (Flesch-Kincaid grade ≤ 10)
- [ ] Every technical term defined in the same sentence it first appears
- [ ] UK English spellings; numbers spelled appropriately (one–nine in flow, digits for 10+ and measurements)
