## 9. Section-by-section content brief

### What the free androgen index actually is (without the textbook)

- Open with the body-feel and the moment, not the formula ([`tone-of-voice.md`](../../02_brand/tone-of-voice.md) Move 1, Section 7 "lead with the body-feel"). The GP printout that said "normal". The mate who mentioned "free T". The private panel with an SHBG line the reader can't read. Phrase the diagnostic-question device **fresh** (the verbatim "I asked him one question" is retired per tone-of-voice Section 2 Move 2 / Section 3).
- Define FAI in plain English: a calculation that estimates the *proportion* of your testosterone that is free and usable, by comparing total testosterone against SHBG. Not a separate hormone: a ratio that tells you how much of the testosterone you have is available to do its job.
- Bridge to the spine: "Your total testosterone is how much is in the tank. Your free androgen index is closer to how much the engine can actually reach."
- **Must avoid:** framing FAI as a diagnosis or as proof of "low testosterone". FAI is a signal that refines the picture; it does not name a condition. Compliance, Section 16.

### Why a "normal" total testosterone can still leave you low

- This is the spine. ~250 words. The VOC Theme A payload, translated.
- Mechanism, plainly: most testosterone in your blood is bound, mostly to SHBG, some to albumin. Bound-to-SHBG testosterone is locked up and unavailable. Only the free (and loosely albumin-bound) fraction can act. If your SHBG is high, more of your total is locked up, so your free T and FAI can be low even when your total number sits comfortably mid-range.
- Name the reader's experience without diagnosing it: this is the gap between "your total is normal" and "you still feel flat", and it is a measurement gap, not necessarily a testosterone-production problem.
- The reframe (tone-of-voice Move 4): "He didn't have a normal-testosterone result. He had an unmeasured-testosterone result." (Writer varies the exact wording; the *device* is the point, not a fixed phrase.)
- Plant the non-duplication flag lightly here, then pay it off in the dedicated H2 below.

### SHBG: the protein that decides how much testosterone you can use

Three H3s. This section carries the `shbg`, `what is shbg`, and `high shbg` cluster.

- **What SHBG does.** SHBG stands for sex hormone binding globulin, a protein made mainly in the liver that binds tightly to testosterone and carries it around in a locked, unavailable form. The more SHBG you have, the more of your testosterone is tied up.
- **What pushes SHBG up (and traps your testosterone).** Ageing, an overactive thyroid, liver conditions, higher oestrogen, and some medications tend to raise SHBG. Higher SHBG means a lower free fraction for the same total. **Mark every one of these mechanisms for source verification at draft time** (NHS + a named endocrinology / SHBG reference); do not assert a specific magnitude without a cite.
- **What pulls SHBG down.** Carrying excess weight, insulin resistance, and an underactive thyroid tend to lower SHBG. A low SHBG can make a total number look lower while the free fraction holds up: the mirror image of the trap, and a reason the FAI is more informative than either number alone. Source-verify at draft time.

### Total, free, bioavailable: three numbers, one hormone

Two H3s. Carries the `what is free testosterone` and `free test testosterone` cluster; mentions the bare `free testosterone` term in passing (row 211, not a target).

- **What is free testosterone.** The small fraction of your testosterone (roughly one to two percent) that is not bound to anything and can act on your tissues. This is the biologically active portion. Define "bioavailable testosterone" in the same breath: free plus the loosely albumin-bound fraction, the part your body can actually get at.
- **What the free androgen index adds (and why albumin is in the calculation).** FAI compares total testosterone to SHBG as a quick index of the free fraction. A directly measured or calculated free testosterone uses total T, SHBG **and albumin** together (the standard calculation method: name it and mark for source verification against a named endocrinology reference at draft time). This is precisely why Kit 1 measures albumin alongside the other four: without it you cannot calculate free T properly. State this as the reason albumin is on the panel, not as a product boast.
- Keep `total vs free testosterone` here as the explained concept, not a ranking claim.

### What counts as a low free androgen index: the UK numbers

The most AI-extractable section. Three H3s. **Every threshold here is marked for source verification at draft time (writer verifies exact ranges and units against a UK private-lab standard + a named endocrinology reference; do NOT ship an unsourced nmol/L or FAI cut-point).**

- **The SHBG reference range.** Give the typical adult-male SHBG reference band in nmol/L as reported by UK labs, flagged for verification. Explain that reference bands vary by lab and assay, which is itself part of the point.
- **The FAI reference range.** Give the typical adult-male FAI band, flagged for verification. Explain FAI is a ratio (usually expressed as a percentage), so it has no units, and that a low FAI with a normal total is the exact pattern this article exists to explain.
- **Why the number is a signal, not a verdict.** A single FAI is a snapshot influenced by timing, recent illness, and assay. It refines the question ("is my usable testosterone low?"); it does not answer "why?" on its own. Sets up the "single result" H2 and the GP-referral H2.

### This isn't the "normal range is a myth" argument (and why both matter)

The other, this article's argument, is that the *wrong number gets measured*: total instead of free, so the reference-range debate never even applies because the usable figure was never on the page.

### Why the time of day you tested changes the whole picture

- ~150 words. Testosterone follows a daily rhythm and is highest in the morning; the standard is a morning sample (typically before 11am, fasting). A number drawn in the afternoon can read lower than the same man's morning value. **Mark the morning-timing claim and any time window for source verification (NHS / endocrinology reference).**
- Why it matters here: a reader dismissed on an afternoon total-T draw may have been measured at his daily low *and* never had his free fraction calculated: two ways the same test can mislead.
- Tie to Kit 1's at-home finger-prick model factually (the reader controls timing), without overclaiming.

### What a single result does and doesn't tell you

- It doesn't, on its own, mean you need TRT. TRT is a clinical decision made by a doctor on the full picture, and (compliance) it is not something Andro Prime offers in Phase 0. Name TRT here **educationally only**: what it is, that it is a prescribed clinical treatment, that this test is not a route to it.
