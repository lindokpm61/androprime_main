ICP 2 — active man 35–50 who trains and tracks his body. He is searching this query because one of:

- **Recovery has slowed.** Soreness lingers longer than it used to. Sleep + diet haven't changed but the body has. He suspects something deeper.

He is NOT:

- A clinician (don't write to a clinician)

## 4. Search-intent decoded

The query reads commercial-but-informational. He's not yet decided where to buy a test — he wants to understand what one would actually tell him. The article must:

- **Answer the literal question** ("what are inflammatory markers, and what does a blood test for them measure") in the opening 40–60 word block
- **Resolve the underlying job** (decide whether to test, what the result would mean, what action it would unlock)
- **Earn the click to Kit 2** by making the test feel like the logical next step — never the only one

## 9. Section-by-section content brief

### What "inflammatory markers" actually means (without the textbook)

- Open with the body-feel cue (soreness that takes longer to go than it used to) — per [blog-ai-seo-strategy.md:103](../blog-ai-seo-strategy.md#L103) audience note.
- Define inflammation in plain English: the immune system's response to irritation, useful in short bursts, draining when it stays elevated.
- Bridge: "Blood doesn't measure how you feel. It measures the chemicals your body releases when it's responding to something. Those chemicals are what an inflammatory markers blood test picks up."
- **Must avoid:** "chronic inflammation is a disease" framing. Compliance — see Section 16.

### The four markers a UK men's blood test would measure

- **hs-CRP (high-sensitivity C-reactive protein):** the headline marker. Explain that "hs" just means a more precise version of the standard CRP test, picking up the lower readings that matter for long-term recovery and cardiovascular risk.
- **Ferritin:** inflammation in disguise. Ferritin rises as an acute-phase reactant, which is why someone with high ferritin and low iron stores can be confusing without context.
- **ESR:** older test, still on NHS panels, but slower-changing and less useful for the active-man use case.
- **IL-6 / fibrinogen:** mention only briefly. Research-grade, not in consumer panels, included for completeness.
- **Must include:** which of these Andro Prime's Kit 2 actually measures (hs-CRP + Ferritin). Be honest about what Kit 2 doesn't measure (ESR, IL-6) and why those omissions are fine for the use case.

### What counts as "high" — the UK numbers

- hs-CRP risk strata: <1.0 mg/L low / 1.0–3.0 mg/L average / >3.0 mg/L elevated (AHA framework, widely adopted by UK private labs).
- Cite the AHA + a UK source (NHS / British Heart Foundation) so the reader sees the number anchored.
- Caveat that a single elevated reading on an acute illness (cold, recent training session, recent dental work) is not the same as a chronic baseline. **Retest in 4–8 weeks** is the actionable line.
- Ferritin: explain the two-direction problem in two sentences. Low ferritin = depleted iron stores (fatigue likely cause). High ferritin can mean inflammation, alcohol load, or — rarely — iron overload. Both ends matter.

### What high inflammation usually means in active men

This is the most important section for time-on-page and AI citation. Lifestyle drivers, framed as a checklist the reader recognises himself in.

- Training load that's outrun recovery — frequency, intensity, deload weeks
- Sleep debt — quality not just hours
- Body composition / visceral fat
- Alcohol consumption
- Ultra-processed food intake
- **The other half:** dental infection, gut issues, undiagnosed autoimmune. **Must include:** the GP-referral line — "If you've checked all the lifestyle drivers and the number still won't come down, that's the conversation to have with your GP, not with us."

### What changes when you actually have the number

- Two-paragraph section. The point of testing isn't the number, it's the loop — baseline → adjust one variable → retest → see what changed.
- Tie to the patient-owned-data V7 thesis: *your data, your record, yours to take to a GP if needed*.
- Hook the Kit 2 retest behaviour without overselling.

### When to see your GP, not us

**This section is non-negotiable for compliance.** ~150 words. Direct, no waffle.

- Any single hs-CRP >10 mg/L → GP same week (acute infection or major inflammatory event)
- Persistently elevated >3 mg/L after 8 weeks of lifestyle adjustment → GP referral
- Unexplained weight loss, night sweats, persistent fever alongside any reading → GP urgently, this isn't a wellness conversation
- Pregnancy / postpartum / under 18 / known chronic condition → GP not us
- **Phrase the section as enabling, not disclaiming.** "We're built for the recovery-aware man who wants to track. We're not built for diagnosis. Here's where the line is."

### How Andro Prime's Energy & Recovery Check measures this

- **One paragraph.** Plain. Connects the article to the product without the article *becoming* the product page.
- Mentions: hs-CRP + Ferritin are 2 of the 4 markers in Kit 2 (the other two: Vitamin D, Active B12). Finger-prick. UKAS ISO 15189-accredited lab. 2–5 working-day turnaround.
- Links to `/lp/energy-recovery` once.
- **Do not duplicate the kit page's biomarker copy.** The LP exists for that.
