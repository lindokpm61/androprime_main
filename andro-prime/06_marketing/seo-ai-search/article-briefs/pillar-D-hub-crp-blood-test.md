## 9. Section-by-section content brief

### What CRP actually is (without the textbook)

- Open with the body-feel cue (the printout, the panel report, the coach's recommendation — the specific moment the reader landed here). Per [`tone-of-voice.md`](../../02_brand/tone-of-voice.md) Move 1 — concrete opener, not abstract claim.
- Define CRP in plain English: a protein your liver releases when your immune system is responding to something. Standard CRP picks up overt inflammation (acute infection, injury, surgery). hs-CRP picks up the low-grade background reading that matters for long-term recovery and cardiovascular risk.
- Bridge: "A CRP blood test measures that protein. It doesn't tell you what's causing it — it tells you it's there, and it tells you how much. What you do with the number is the next conversation."
- **Must avoid:** treating CRP as a diagnosis. CRP is a signal, not a verdict. Compliance — see Section 16.

### Why your GP ordered it — or why your panel includes it

- Short H2 — ~150 words. The reader often landed here because someone else ordered the test. Acknowledge that without making him feel patronised.
- Common GP triggers: persistent fatigue work-up, pre-op screening, suspected infection, joint pain investigation, autoimmune screen.
- Common private-panel triggers: comprehensive health check, men's health panel, athlete recovery panel, inflammation marker explicitly requested.
- Set up the next H2 by naming the question the reader actually has: "OK, but what does my number mean?"

### What counts as "high" — the UK numbers

This is the most-cited section per the GEO + AI-extraction model. Three nested H3s.

- **Under 5 mg/L — the NHS clinical cutoff.** NHS labs typically report "<5 mg/L = normal" for standard CRP. That cutoff was set to flag obvious inflammation (infection, autoimmune flare) — it is not designed to differentiate the "average" from the "low-grade elevated" recovery-aware reader cares about.
- **hs-CRP risk strata.** Under 1.0 mg/L = low cardiovascular risk; 1.0–3.0 mg/L = average; over 3.0 mg/L = elevated. The 2003 AHA/CDC Scientific Statement (Pearson et al., *Circulation*) — adopted by UK private labs as the de-facto standard. NHS clinical guidance references the same cutoffs for cardiovascular risk stratification.
- **How "normal CRP" and "low hs-CRP" mean different things.** A standard CRP of 2 mg/L would print "normal" on an NHS report but sits in the "average to elevated" band on the hs-CRP framework. The test name on the requisition matters. Most consumer panels run hs-CRP — the more precise version — by default. State which one Andro Prime's Kit 2 runs (hs-CRP).
- **Acute confounders:** recent cold, hard training session, dental work, recent vaccination, pregnancy. Any of these can push CRP up temporarily. **A single elevated reading on an acute event is not a baseline.** Retest in 4–8 weeks is the actionable line.

### What high CRP usually means in active men

This section is where the article earns time-on-page and AI citations. Five H3s; framed as a checklist the reader recognises himself in.

- **Recent illness or injury — the acute signal.** Cold, flu, dental abscess, surgery, broken bone, even a hard race. CRP responds to these and settles in 1–2 weeks. Most "surprise high CRP" reads land here.
- **Training load that's outrun recovery.** Frequency, intensity, missing deload weeks. Cite Cerqueira et al. 2020 *Frontiers in Physiology* (same source the Pillar G hub uses) for the elevated baseline in high-volume training.
- **Sleep debt.** Quality, not just hours. Cite Irwin et al. 2016 *Biological Psychiatry* meta-analysis on sleep duration and inflammation.
- **Body composition / visceral fat / alcohol / ultra-processed food.** Group these in one paragraph — body-comp drivers run together in research and in practice.
- **The other half — when it isn't lifestyle.** Dental infection, gut issues, undiagnosed autoimmune (rheumatoid, IBD), chronic kidney disease, less commonly cancer. **Must include the GP-referral line** verbatim from the Pillar G hub pattern: "If you've checked the lifestyle drivers and the number won't come down, that's the conversation to have with your GP, not with us."

### What a single high reading does NOT tell you

Short H2 — ~200 words. This is the defuse-the-worry section. Critical for the worried-well subset of the reader pool.

- **It doesn't diagnose anything.** CRP is a marker, not a diagnosis. A high reading doesn't name a cause. A doctor uses it as part of a picture, not in isolation.
- **It doesn't predict short-term risk on its own.** hs-CRP improves cardiovascular risk prediction when added to other markers — it doesn't replace them.
- **It doesn't tell you about cancer.** High CRP can occur with cancer, but the reverse — high CRP = cancer — is not how the marker works. Most high CRP has a benign cause. Persistently very high CRP (>10 mg/L on retest, with weight loss / night sweats / persistent fever) is a GP escalation. Single high reading in an otherwise well man is almost always lifestyle or recent infection.

### What changes when you actually have the number (baseline → retest)

- ~250 words. The Pillar D version of the patient-owned-data V7 thesis ([brand-description.md v1.1](../../02_brand/brand-description.md)). The point of testing isn't the number, it's the loop — baseline → adjust one variable → retest → see what changed.
- Pillar D speaks the medical-literate version of the loop: marker → mg/L → retest at 4–8 weeks → delta → next decision.
- Link out to Pillar F hub (patient-owned-data GEO flagship) once it exists. Until then, prefetch={false}.

### When to see your GP, not us

**Non-negotiable for compliance.** ~180 words. Direct, no waffle. Pillar D version of the Pillar G hub Section 9 GP-referral block, scaled for the worried-well risk.

- Any single CRP >10 mg/L on a non-acute baseline → GP same week
- Persistently >3 mg/L hs-CRP across two retests 8 weeks apart with no lifestyle cause identified → GP referral
- Any reading + unexplained weight loss / night sweats / persistent fever → GP urgently
- Pregnancy / postpartum / under 18 / known chronic condition → GP not us
- **Phrase as enabling, not disclaiming** ([`tone-of-voice.md`](../../02_brand/tone-of-voice.md) Section 7 — "name the boundary, don't apologise for it").
