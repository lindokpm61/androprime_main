## 9. Section-by-section content brief

### Before the list — what counts as a "sign"

- ~120 words. Sets the frame so the listicle doesn't read as a diagnostic instrument.
- "Signs aren't diagnoses. They're body-feel cues that turn up more often in men with low Vitamin D than in men with adequate levels. None of the 14 signs below on its own means you're deficient. A handful of them clustering together is worth checking — and a blood test is the only way to actually find out."

### The 14 signs

Each H3 = 60-100 words. Three-beat structure per item:

1. **Body-feel cue** (concrete, ICP 2 recognises himself)
2. **Why it shows up** (one sentence of mechanism, plain English)
3. **The honest qualifier** (one sentence of "other causes also do this, so..." — this is what stops the article from over-claiming and is the Keith-voice differentiator vs the chemist-4-u listicle)

**Item-by-item direction:**

1. **Tiredness that doesn't shift with sleep.** You sleep 7-8 hours and wake up flat. Vitamin D plays a role in cellular energy metabolism in muscle. Tiredness has many causes — sleep quality, iron, thyroid, training load — so this is suggestive, not diagnostic.
2. **Low mood through the dark months.** October hits, the mood drops a notch by January. Tracks UK latitude. Not every winter mood drop is Vitamin D — but if yours started worsening in your late 30s, it's worth checking.
3. **Slow recovery from training.** Sessions you'd shake off in a day now take two. Vitamin D contributes to normal muscle function. Training load and sleep are the other big drivers — if both are honest and recovery is still slow, low Vitamin D is a plausible third cause.
4. **Bone ache you can't pin on a session.** A dull ache in shins, hips or lower back without a specific cause. At the severe end (under 25 nmol/L) this is osteomalacia — GP territory. At the milder end, low-grade bone ache is one of the most under-recognised Vitamin D signals.
5. **Muscle weakness in legs and arms.** Stairs feel heavier. The weight you'd press easily feels harder. Vitamin D supports neuromuscular function; deficiency shows up as proximal muscle weakness in clinical literature.
6. **More colds and flu than usual.** You used to get one a winter, now you're getting three. Martineau et al. 2017 *BMJ* meta-analysis found Vitamin D supplementation reduced acute respiratory infection risk, with the biggest effect in deficient people.
7. **Lower back pain that creeps up.** Not from lifting badly, not from sitting poorly — just a baseline ache that wasn't there last year. Observational link to low Vitamin D; mechanism is mixed (bone, muscle, inflammation).
8. **Hair shedding heavier than the seasonal norm.** Some seasonal shed is normal. Persistent heavier shedding in men with adequate iron and thyroid sometimes tracks low Vitamin D, with hair follicle Vitamin D receptors as the proposed mechanism.
9. **Slow-healing cuts and grazes.** Wounds that used to close in 3 days now take 5 or 6. Vitamin D plays a role in skin barrier and immune function during healing. Severe deficiency carries clearer evidence here than mild.
10. **Joint stiffness, especially morning.** First 10-15 minutes after waking are stiffer than they used to be. Inflammatory mechanisms are plausible; the same caveats apply about other causes (training load, age, hydration).
11. **Headaches without obvious cause.** Not migraines, not dehydration headaches — a low-grade head ache pattern that wasn't there before. Cohort data is weak; cite as suggestive rather than well-established.
12. **Concentration and mental fog.** You re-read the same paragraph three times. Decision fatigue lands earlier. Vitamin D receptors exist in brain tissue; the mechanism stories are interesting; the clinical evidence is still building.
13. **Unrefreshing sleep.** 7 hours that feels like 5. Vitamin D's role in sleep regulation is an active research area — too early to call it established, fair to note the association.
14. **No signs at all (the one most UK men miss).** The frame-flip. Vitamin D deficiency runs silent for years in most UK men. By the time symptoms are unmistakable, you've been deficient for a long time. The most accurate "sign" of low Vitamin D in the UK adult male population is being a UK adult male in the dark half of the year. The test is the only honest answer.

### What to do if you recognise yourself in this list

- ~150 words. The pivot from listicle to action.
- Three steps: (a) note the cluster — one sign is noise, three or four together is signal; (b) check it with a blood test (Vitamin D specifically, alongside the other recovery markers worth running at the same time); (c) act on the number — supplementation for the middle band, GP for the deficient band, retest at 8-12 weeks. Link UP to A.hub for the full thresholds and what-to-do mechanics.
