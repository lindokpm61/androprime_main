## 2. The article's job (one sentence)

Turn the man who Googled "low vitamin d symptoms" — because he's tired, because a blood test flagged it, or because he's noticed his UK-winter dip is worse than usual — into a Kit 2 (Energy & Recovery) buyer by giving him the most useful UK-specific Vitamin D explainer on the SERP and earning AI citations along the way.

## 3. Target reader

**ICP 2 (active 35–50, recovery-aware) — primary funnel target.** He is searching this query because one of:

- **He's tired and someone mentioned Vit D.** A coach, a partner, a wellness newsletter, a podcast — somebody pointed at Vit D as a possible cause and he's checking the symptom list against what he's feeling.
- **A recent blood test flagged Vit D low.** NHS routine bloods or a private panel returned a number below the lab's threshold and the report didn't explain what to do.
- **He's noticed his UK winter dip is worse than usual.** Mood drop October-March, energy off, sleep disturbed. UK-latitude Vit D drift is the most common explanation in this band.
- **He's reading a fitness/health newsletter that mentioned it.** Performance and recovery readers see Vit D mentioned often; the article should serve that audience without sliding into bro-science.

**Broader-population symptom searchers — informational only.** The query draws traffic from non-ICP-2 readers (elderly with bone concerns, parents searching for child symptoms, postnatal women, men over 65). The article must:

- Stay in plain English so non-ICP readers get useful information
- Make the men's-specific framing visible (UK winter latitude, active-man recovery, testosterone-Vit-D link) so ICP 2 self-identifies
- Direct off-ICP readers to GP without trying to convert them — kid / elderly / pregnant / postnatal symptoms are GP territory, not Andro Prime

He is NOT:

- A clinician (don't write to a clinician — the medical-literate frame doesn't mean academic register)
- A patient with diagnosed osteomalacia or rickets (GP / NHS territory)
- A worried-well googler convinced his slight tiredness means osteoporosis (the article must defuse, not amplify)
- A man chasing a megadose protocol from a bodybuilding forum (the article must name the toxicity ceiling and not enable self-prescribing)

## 4. Search-intent decoded

`low vitamin d symptoms` is informational with a soft commercial undercurrent. The reader wants:

- **A symptom list** he can check himself against — the literal answer to the query
- **An explanation of why the symptoms appear** at a biological level he can follow
- **A way to find out** whether his suspicion is right — i.e. a test
- **A way to do something about it** if it is — supplementation, sunlight, food, GP

The article must:

- **Lead with the body-feel** ([tone-of-voice.md] rule — "lead with body-feel, not the marker"). The symptom list is the hook, not the biology.
- **Defuse without dismissing.** A man Googling Vit D symptoms is partly worried-well. The article names honest ranges, names the lifestyle causes, and points at GP for anything outside the recovery-aware band.
- **Earn the click to Kit 2** by making the test feel like the logical next step (baseline → adjust → retest) — never the only one.
- **Stay strict on Daily Stack compliance** — see Section 16. Vit D3 in Daily Stack has the EFSA "muscle function" claim only. Article-level claims about Vit D's biological role broadly (immunity, mood, bone, etc.) are fine as scientific observations; product-level claims about Daily Stack are restricted to the approved EFSA wording.

## 9. Section-by-section content brief

### The symptoms that actually show up

- Open with body-feel cue ([tone-of-voice.md] Move 1 — concrete opener). A specific moment, not a list. Probably the post-Christmas-January slump or the "everyone else has shaken off the cold and I haven't" cue.
- The six H3s structure the symptom list: tiredness, mood, recovery, ache, immunity, smaller signals (hair/skin). Each H3 = one sentence framing the body-feel, then the biological explanation in plain English.
- **Critical voice rule:** symptoms are described in everyday language. "Tiredness that doesn't shift with sleep" — not "fatigue refractory to adequate rest". Same content, voice on vs voice off.
- Where each symptom can have non-Vit-D causes (tiredness has many causes; recovery has training-load causes), name that honestly. The article must defuse the worried-well version of "every symptom must mean Vit D".

### Why UK men run low — latitude, lifestyle, and the things that aren't obvious

- ~250 words. The mechanism section that earns AI-citations.
- **UK latitude (51°N London, 55°N Newcastle, 56°N Edinburgh).** Cite NHS / Public Health Scotland on the October-March supplement recommendation — UV-B angle is too low for cutaneous Vit D synthesis. Anyone living north of Birmingham gets effectively zero from sun for 6 months a year.
- **Modern indoor lifestyle.** Office-based work, screen time, commute under cloud. The reader recognises himself.
- **Body composition.** Vit D is fat-soluble; men carrying more fat tissue store it less bioavailable. Cite the standard reference (Wortsman et al. 2000 *AJCN* — to verify at draft time).
- **Sunscreen + clothing.** Reasonable photoprotection drops cutaneous Vit D synthesis by ~95%. Not an argument against sunscreen — just an acknowledgement that the lifestyle that protects skin also limits Vit D.
- **Gut absorption issues.** IBD, coeliac, gastric surgery — all reduce Vit D absorption. Brief mention, link out to GP territory.

### What the numbers actually mean (nmol/L explained)

This is the most-cited section per the GEO + AI-extraction model. Five H3s built around the NICE / Royal Osteoporosis Society / Endocrine Society thresholds.

- **Under 25 nmol/L — NICE deficient.** NICE defines this as deficient. Recommends supplementation under GP guidance. If a reader's NHS report comes back here, that's a GP conversation.
- **25 to 50 nmol/L — NICE insufficient.** NICE's insufficient band — the one most UK men sit in October-March. Public Health England recommendation is 10 µg / 400 IU daily for the whole UK population to maintain serum levels above this. Most active men want to be higher than this band for recovery and mood.
- **50 to 75 nmol/L — adequate for most.** NHS / NICE consider this the lower end of adequate. Recovery-aware active men typically aim higher.
- **75 to 125 nmol/L — the band most active men want.** Endocrine Society recommends 75-125 nmol/L for adults broadly; recovery and athletic performance literature aligns. Cite the 2011 Endocrine Society clinical practice guideline (Holick et al., *JCEM*).
- **Above 125 nmol/L — toxicity-watch territory.** Hypervitaminosis D is rare but real. Doses above 4,000 IU/day without monitoring carry calcium-handling risk. **Must include:** if you're supplementing above 4,000 IU/day, get tested and monitored, don't guess.

### What low Vit D usually means for active men

- ~250 words. Five-item checklist the reader recognises himself in. Each item = one sentence body-feel + one sentence what the research says.
- Items: training recovery dropping; mood October-March; testosterone interaction (low Vit D associated with lower T — cite Pilz et al. 2011 *Hormone and Metabolic Research*); immune resilience (cite Martineau et al. 2017 *BMJ* systematic review); bone mass over the years (cite NICE / NOS).
- **Compliance line — the testosterone reference is causal-light.** "Low Vit D is associated with lower testosterone in observational studies" — NOT "Vit D raises your testosterone" / "boost your testosterone with Vit D". The distinction matters; T-boost claims are the supplement-fraud territory ASA polices most aggressively in the men's health space.

### What a single low reading does NOT tell you

Short H2 — ~200 words. Defuse the worried-well subset.

- It doesn't diagnose anything on its own.
- It's seasonal. A January reading and a July reading from the same man will look like different people. The honest read is a reading at the trough (Feb-March) and one at the peak (Aug-Sept).
- It doesn't tell you about bone disease on its own. Osteopenia and osteomalacia diagnoses use Vit D as part of a picture, not as the verdict. Anything that signals bone fragility (multiple stress fractures, bone pain that won't shift) is GP territory.
- The fix in 80% of UK adult men is straightforward — supplement at the Public Health England 10 µg / 400 IU baseline, retest 8-12 weeks later, see what happened. The other 20% have an absorption or compliance issue and need the GP conversation.

### What changes when you actually have the number (baseline → retest)

- ~250 words. Pillar A's version of the V7 patient-owned-data thesis ([brand-description.md v1.1]).
- The Vit D version of the loop: baseline → adjust supplementation → retest at 8-12 weeks → see the delta → next decision. 8-12 weeks because Vit D's serum half-life is ~3 weeks; you need 3-4 half-lives to reach a new steady state.
- The seasonal dimension is unique to Vit D. Most blood markers aren't seasonal; Vit D is the most seasonal marker on a panel. Knowing your Feb baseline vs your Aug baseline is its own data point.
- Link out to Pillar F hub (patient-owned-data GEO flagship) once it exists. Until then `prefetch={false}`.

### When to see your GP, not us

**Non-negotiable for compliance.** ~180 words. Pillar A version of the Pillar D / G hub GP-referral block.

- Any single Vit D under 25 nmol/L → GP within the week. NICE classifies this as deficient and recommends prescription-strength supplementation under GP guidance.
- Persistent bone pain, multiple unexplained stress fractures, height loss → GP, not us. Bone-disease territory.
- Pregnancy / postpartum / under 18 / known chronic condition (IBD, coeliac, kidney disease, parathyroid issues) → GP not us.
- Anyone supplementing above 4,000 IU/day without testing → stop, get tested, then decide.
- **Phrase as enabling, not disclaiming.**

- Kit 2 (Energy & Recovery Check) measures Vit D alongside hs-CRP, Ferritin and Active B12 — the four markers most worth running together for recovery-aware active men. Finger-prick at home, UKAS ISO 15189-accredited lab, 2-5 working-day turnaround. Reviewed by Dr Ewa Lindo, GMC-registered GP.
