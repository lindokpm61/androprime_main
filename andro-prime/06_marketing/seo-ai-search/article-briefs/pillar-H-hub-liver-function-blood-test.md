## 2. The article's job (one sentence)

Turn the man who Googled "liver function blood test" — because a GP printout flagged a number, a private panel
returned an ALT/GGT value he doesn't understand, or he's an active man who drinks socially and trains hard and
wants a baseline — into a **waitlist signup** (and, on kit launch, a Liver-kit / Kit 3 Plus buyer) by giving
him the most useful UK men-specific LFT explainer on the SERP and earning AI citations along the way.

## 3. Target reader

**ICP 2 (active 35–50, recovery-aware) — primary.** He landed here because:
- **A GP printout flagged a liver number** (often part of a routine or fatigue work-up) and he wants to
  understand it without going back to the surgery.
- **A private panel returned an ALT / GGT / ALP value** the report didn't explain in plain language.
- **He drinks socially and trains hard** and has half-wondered what that does to his liver markers — the
  active-man self-audit no incumbent speaks to.

**ICP 3 (preventative 40+) — secondary.** Running a comprehensive screen, wants to know what each liver marker
means before booking.

He is NOT: a clinician; a patient with diagnosed liver disease, hepatitis, or cirrhosis (compliance — route to
GP, never write to that audience); or a worried-well googler convinced a mildly raised ALT means liver failure
(defuse, don't amplify — Section 16).

## 4. Search-intent decoded

`liver function blood test` reads informational-with-commercial-intent. The reader has usually already met the
marker (printout, panel, recommendation) and wants the explainer. The article must:
- **Answer the literal question** (what an LFT measures, what each marker is, what counts as high) in the
  opening 40–60 word block.
- **Resolve the underlying job** (interpret a specific number, decide whether it's lifestyle or something to
  act on, decide whether to retest or see a GP).
- **Defuse worry honestly** — "raised liver enzymes" intent is partly worried-well; give honest ranges, name
  the benign drivers (recent heavy training, alcohol, fatty liver, medications), name the GP-referral lines.
- **Earn the waitlist signup** by making a baseline feel like the logical next step (baseline → adjust → retest).

## 9. Section-by-section content brief

### What a liver function test actually measures
- Open with the body-feel cue (the printout, the panel value, the active-man self-audit). Concrete opener
  (tone-of-voice Move 1).
- Plain-English on the six markers. Critical men's-angle point: **ALT/AST are not liver-damage verdicts** — a
  hard training block can raise them transiently (muscle contributes AST; intense exercise raises ALT too).
  This is the single most useful thing the SERP doesn't say to active men.
- GGT: factual on its sensitivity to alcohol and enzyme-inducing drugs — **neutral, never moralising**.

### Why your GP ordered it — or why your panel includes it
- ~150 words. Common GP triggers (routine screen, fatigue work-up, medication monitoring, alcohol review).
  Common private-panel triggers (comprehensive check, athlete panel). Acknowledge without patronising.

### What counts as "normal" — the UK ranges
- Most-cited section (GEO/AI extraction). Give the standard UK lab ranges for ALT, AST, ALP, GGT, bilirubin,
  albumin, each sourced. State plainly that **lab ranges vary slightly between labs** and that "normal" is a
  population band, not a personal optimum (link to the Pillar C `myth-of-normal-range` spoke — sibling-pillar
  cross-link).
- Acute confounders: a hard session in the last 48h, a heavy weekend, a recent viral illness, new
  medication/supplement. **A single reading after any of these is not a baseline.** Retest in 4–8 weeks.

### What a raised result usually means in active men
- The time-on-page + AI-citation section. Five H3s as a checklist the reader recognises:
  - **Training block** — cite the exercise-and-aminotransferases evidence (e.g. Pettersson et al. 2008,
    *BMC* — strenuous exercise raises ALT/AST for days). Writer verifies at draft.
  - **Alcohol** — UK CMO 14-units guidance referenced neutrally; GGT/AST-ALT-ratio context, no shaming.
  - **Fatty liver (MASLD)** — common in men 40+, often symptomless; name it, cite prevalence, route lifestyle
    + GP, never claim Andro Prime detects or treats it.
  - **Medications and supplements** — paracetamol, statins, some bodybuilding/"liver support" supplements.
    **Must NOT claim any supplement protects/heals the liver** (no milk-thistle claims; EFSA rules; guardrail).
  - **The other half — when it isn't lifestyle** — viral hepatitis, autoimmune, biliary issues. **GP-referral
    line verbatim:** "If you've checked the lifestyle drivers and the numbers won't settle, that's the
    conversation to have with your GP, not with us."

### What a single abnormal reading does NOT tell you
- ~200 words. Defuse-the-worry section. A mildly raised ALT is common and usually benign; LFTs are a pattern,
  read together, over time — not a single-number verdict; an abnormal LFT is not a diagnosis of liver disease.

### What changes when you actually have the number (baseline → retest)
- ~250 words. The patient-owned-data loop in liver terms: baseline → adjust one variable (a dry month, a
  deload) → retest at 4–8 weeks → read the delta. Link to Pillar F once live.

### When to see your GP, not us
- **Non-negotiable.** ~180 words. Direct. Any of: jaundice (yellow skin/eyes), dark urine/pale stools,
  persistent right-upper abdominal pain, markedly raised enzymes (e.g. ALT > 3× upper limit), any abnormal
  result with feeling unwell → GP, urgently for the symptomatic ones. Known liver condition / pregnancy →
  GP not us. Framed as enabling, not disclaiming (tone-of-voice §7).

### How Andro Prime will measure this — join the waitlist
- **One paragraph. This is the email-capture section — the D hub's "How we measure it / Kit 2" equivalent, but
  there is no live product.** State plainly: a liver panel is on the Andro Prime roadmap (Liver kit / Kit 3
  Plus); join the waitlist to be first and to get a founding-customer offer. **No fake "buy now."** Finger-prick
  at home, UKAS-accredited lab, GMC-GP-reviewed recommendation logic — framed as what's coming.
