### 0:00 — Cold open (a man, a quote, the question)

**[VISUAL]** Keith to camera, plain room, window light. No logo yet.

**CAM:** "A man came to me last year. Fit bloke. Trained four times a week, ate well, did the lot.

He told me his body had stopped bouncing back. The session he'd shake off in a day was taking three. The ache after the gym hung around. Same sleep. Same food. But something had gone.

He'd been to his GP. Bloods came back. 'All normal.'

I asked him one question.

What did they actually test?

He didn't know. He had the appointment. He had the 'you're fine'. He never had the numbers.

That's nearly every man I talk to. He didn't have a recovery problem. He had a problem nobody had measured."

**[TEXT]** Inflammation: what it is, what's normal, what to do.

### 0:45 — What inflammation actually is

**CAM:** "So let's deal with the word first. Inflammation.

Think of it as your body's alarm. Cut your finger, catch a cold, do a hard session, the alarm goes off, sorts it, switches back off. That's it doing its job.

The problem isn't the alarm. It's the alarm that never switches off.

It just sits there. Low. In the background. For weeks. You can't see it, you can't feel it directly, but it quietly pulls down your energy and how fast you recover, in men who look completely fine on paper.

A blood test can't measure how tired you feel. What it can do is tell you the alarm's been left on."

### 1:30 — The two numbers

**[VISUAL]** On-screen: hs-CRP, ferritin highlighted; ESR greyed.

**CAM:** "There are two numbers worth knowing here. That's it. Two.

The first's got an ugly name. hs-CRP. Forget the letters. It's your smoke alarm reading. When something's irritating your system, your liver puts this out, and the test measures how much. The 'high sensitivity' bit just means it catches the quiet smoulder, not only the house fire. That's the one you want.

The second is ferritin. Its day job is storing iron. But it's got a second job most men never hear about. It climbs when there's inflammation around. So you can read high on ferritin while your actual iron is low. Same number. Two stories. Which is exactly why it needs reading in context.

You might also see ESR on an older NHS test. That's just the slower, older version of the same idea. Not wrong. Just not the sharp one."

### 2:30 — What counts as high

**[VISUAL]** StatBox graphic, three bands, traffic-light colours.

**CAM:** "Right. What's a good number.

Think traffic lights.

Under 1, you're green. That's where you want to sit.

1 to 3, amber. Common. Not a crisis. Not clean either.

Over 3, that's worth asking why, and worth testing again.

Over 10, that's different. That's see your GP this week, because a number that high usually means something active, not just a rough few months."

### 3:15 — Why one reading lies

**CAM:** "One warning, and it matters.

A single reading can fool you.

Test the morning after a brutal leg day. Or the week you've got a cold. Or just after the dentist. The number reads high, and none of it's your normal.

So here's the rule. Take a reading. Leave it four to eight weeks. Test again when you're not ill and haven't smashed yourself the day before.

The number you've watched move. That's the one that means something."

### 4:00 — What's pushing it up

**[VISUAL]** B-roll: training, a clock, a pint, a takeaway.

**CAM:** "So your number's up. In a healthy man between 35 and 50, it's almost never anything dramatic. It's usually one of four ordinary things. Sometimes two of them stacked.

One. Training harder than you're recovering. Volume creeps up. Easy weeks get skipped. The body never catches up. A bit sore after a session is normal. Still high on a rest week is the warning.

Two. Sleep. Run under six hours for a few weeks and this climbs. It's not a soft factor. It shows up in the blood.

Three. Belly fat. Not the bit you can pinch. The fat round your organs. That fat isn't just sitting there, it actually pumps these alarm signals out. Good news, it's also the quickest to respond.

Four. Booze and processed food. The two easiest to change. The two everyone ignores."

**[TEXT]** Sources: NHS, British Heart Foundation, AHA/CDC, Frontiers in Physiology (2020), Biological Psychiatry (2016). Full list in description.

### 5:15 — The honest bit

**[VISUAL]** Keith to camera, slower, direct.

**CAM:** "Now the honest bit. This doesn't change.

We're here for the man who wants to keep an eye on his own body. We're not here to diagnose you. So I'll tell you straight where the line is.

See your GP this week if your number's over 10. Or if you've got weight dropping off you for no reason, night sweats, or a fever that won't shift.

See them soon if it stays above 3 after a couple of honest months of fixing the basics. Or if your iron's low.

I'd rather lose the sale than have you watching me when you should be sat in front of your doctor."

### 6:15 — Why test at all

**CAM:** "So why bother testing.

Because without a starting number, you're guessing.

You add a fish oil. Drop the lunchtime pint. Sleep a bit more for a fortnight. And you've honestly no idea if any of it did a thing.

With a starting number, you change one thing. Give it a couple of months. Test again. Now you can see what moved.

That's the whole game. And it's yours. If you ever do take it to a doctor, you walk in with a story over time, not one lonely reading."
