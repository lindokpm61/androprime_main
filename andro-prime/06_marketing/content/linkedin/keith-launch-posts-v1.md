**Recommended order:**

1. Founder story — *post first.* Establishes who Keith is.
2. "My GP told me my testosterone was normal" — the most clickable hook.
3. 9 biomarkers your GP doesn't run — practical, shareable.
4. The NHS gap — the bigger argument; positions the brand.
5. Introducing Dr Ewa Lindo — the credibility anchor; primes for launch.

Posts run 1,400–2,200 characters each (well under LinkedIn's 3,000-char limit; long enough to read substantive). Each ends with a soft CTA — a question or an invitation, never "buy now."

For each post: review, adjust to your actual voice, post manually. **Don't schedule all five at the same time** — LinkedIn's algorithm penalises burst-posting from new-active accounts.

## POST 3 — 9 biomarkers (shareable practical post)

**Suggested posting day:** Week -3, Monday.
**Hook angle:** Practical, useful. The "save this post" type.

> Nine markers your GP probably won't run, and what each one is actually telling you.
>
> Total testosterone. The headline number. Useful, but on its own incomplete — the NHS reference range was set to spot illness, not optimisation.
>
> Free testosterone. The fraction of testosterone that is actually biologically available. You can have a "normal" total T and a poor free T. Your GP won't usually run this.
>
> SHBG. The protein that binds testosterone and dictates how much free T you actually get. Often the missing piece when free T is unexpectedly low.
>
> Vitamin D. Around 40% of UK men over 40 are below the level that supports normal immune and muscle function. Your GP runs it sometimes. They probably haven't run yours.
>
> Active B12. Different to the standard B12 test — measures the form your body can actually use. Low levels show up as fatigue, foggy thinking, slow recovery.
>
> Ferritin. Your iron stores. Low ferritin is the most common reason men in their 40s are quietly knackered. The NHS rarely tests it without a specific complaint.
>
> hs-CRP. Inflammation. Chronically elevated CRP changes how your body recovers from training, sleep, even mild illness. Almost nobody runs this on a routine check.
>
> HbA1c. Your three-month average blood glucose. Tells you whether you're walking into pre-diabetes long before a fasting glucose would catch it.
>
> Cholesterol panel. Total, HDL, LDL, triglycerides. Your GP runs the headline number. The ratios are where the actual story is.
>
> The point is not that any one number means something on its own. The point is that the conversation about how a man feels at 40 is being held with three of these numbers when it should be held with all nine.
>
> Andro Prime tests the right ones. Plain-English report. £119 for the four energy and recovery markers. £179 for all nine. Launching in seven weeks.
>
> Save this for when your GP next says you're fine.

**Char count:** ~2,090.
**Compliance notes:** Each marker described factually, no diagnostic claims about specific conditions. "Almost nobody runs this on a routine check" is a description of standard NHS practice, not a criticism. Pricing referenced cleanly (£119, £179) — no discount language. No TRT, no ashwagandha.

> A GP gets ten minutes per appointment.
>
> In those ten minutes they have to listen to you, look at you, decide if you're ill, and do something about it.
>
> They do not have ten minutes to optimise you.
>
> This is not a criticism of GPs. It's a description of the job. The NHS was built to spot disease, intervene, and discharge you. It is the best system in the world at that job. It is also a system that, by design, cannot tell a 42-year-old man whose testosterone has slid quietly from 22 to 11 nmol/L over a decade that anything is wrong.

> The private clinic charges £200 for the consultation alone, before any tests. Then another £150 for the tests. Then asks you to come back for results.
>
> What if there was a kit. £119. UKAS-accredited lab. Same markers. GP-interpreted report. Specific recommendation based on your actual numbers. Posted to your house.
>
> That's Andro Prime. Launching in seven weeks. UK only.
>
> If you've ever sat in a GP's office and walked out thinking "but I'm not fine" — this is for you.

**Char count:** ~1,640.
**Compliance notes:** Pro-NHS framing ("not a criticism") avoids regulator/political backlash. The £200 / £150 / £119 comparison frames premium pricing favourably. No diagnostic claims. No TRT availability. No ashwagandha.

## What's NOT in this set (intentionally)

These five posts cover the ICP 1 (Symptomatic Achiever) and ICP 2 (Proactive Optimiser) hooks. We have NOT yet written:

- ICP 3 hook ("I want a baseline before I get older") — different audience, different post. Add post-launch when there's a customer testimonial to anchor it.
- ICP 4 (biohacker / high-performance) hook — defer until peptide tier scoping is real, post-CQC.
- Behind-the-scenes / build-in-public content — useful in the launch window but secondary to the founder-credibility-product trio above.
- Customer language posts (verbatim user quotes) — not yet — no customers.

The launch sequence above is deliberately founder-led: Keith establishes himself, Ewa establishes the clinical foundation, then product details slot in once both anchors exist. Reverse order — leading with product features — is the standard mistake new founders make on LinkedIn and converts substantially worse.
