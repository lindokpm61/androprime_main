## 1. The principle in one line

**People search, feel, and buy in feeling-language, not clinical language. So every piece of content, on every channel, leads with the feeling, reveals the data as the answer, and lets the explanation be the product.**

The blood test, the biomarker, the number: these are the *answer*, never the *hook*.

---

## 2. Why this is a rule, not a hunch

Two independent bodies of evidence point the same way.

### A. The search demand (decontaminated DFS pull, 2026-06-26)

A fresh 1,120-keyword pull, collapsed to 610 distinct demand clusters (removing DataForSEO's variant-inflation, where one head term like "crp blood test" returns 11 near-duplicate spellings each carrying full volume) and stripped of NHS-booking, drug-side-effect, and brand noise:

- **Feeling/experiential language has the breadth: 275 distinct clusters vs 133 clinical (2:1).** People describe their problem in a huge variety of plain, first-person phrasings.
- **Clinical language has concentration, not breadth:** its volume comes from ~10 generic head terms ("blood test", "crp", "fbc", "liver function test", "testosterone test") that are NHS-shadowed and price-compared commodities. Whoever searches "blood test" is one tab from a free NHS draw and a £30 competitor.
- **The voice signature of real demand:** 24% are questions (why/how/what), 20% are first-person (I/my/am I), 28% carry "all the time / always / constant." The register is *chronic, self-referential, seeking an explanation*.
- **The literal words people use:** "why am I always tired", "always tired", "brain fog", "stressed", "no energy", "can't sleep", "low mood", "low libido", "weight gain". This is the vocabulary.

**Conclusion:** the clinical-commodity terms are a price-compressed, contested trap. The feeling doors are the broad, uncontested, premium-amenable surface.

### B. The buying psychology (the "explanation premium")

Why feeling-led content converts at a premium, evidenced:

- **Diagnosis-relief:** receiving a label/explanation for how you feel is itself a relief, independent of treatment. The test sells the relief of an answer, not accuracy.
- **Processing fluency:** information that is *easy to understand* is judged more true, more trustworthy, and higher quality. Clarity, not clinical depth, builds the premium perception. Dense, savvy explanations *lower* perceived quality for a non-clinical audience.
- **Price-placebo + emotion:** premium price and look-feel measurably increase perceived efficacy; emotional, uncertainty-resolving purchases carry materially higher willingness to pay.

**Conclusion:** a customer arriving via a feeling ("why am I exhausted") is buying *the answer to why they feel broken* — an emotional, uncontested purchase with no commodity comparison. That is where the premium lives.

---

## 3. The universal rule (every channel)

Three beats, in this order, always:

1. **Mirror the feeling** in the reader's own words. ("Tired all the time. Not sleepy. Flat.")
2. **Reveal the data** as the answer to the feeling. ("That's what a ferritin test picks up.")
3. **The explanation is the value.** The clarity of the answer is the product, and the premium justification.

Clinical vocabulary (biomarkers, panels, ranges) lives in beats 2 and 3. **Never beat 1.** If a headline, subject line, or ad hook leads with a biomarker or "blood test", it has broken the rule.

## 4. Per-channel application

| Channel | Entry hook leads with | The clinical content's job | Don't |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Blog — feeling hubs** | the feeling, in search-verbatim language ("why am I always tired") | the reveal + the explanation, mid-article | don't open with a biomarker definition |
| **Blog — biomarker spokes** | bridge to the feeling within the first screen, then define | own the featured-snippet answer, then funnel UP to the feeling hub | don't let a dry definition be the whole top; don't strand the spoke without a link to its feeling hub |
| **Newsletter** | the feeling as the subject line ("Why you're tired all the time") | the body explainer + the relief | never "Your ferritin levels" / "New blood test" as a subject |
| **Meta / paid ads** | the feeling (also the policy-safe lane: structure-function, no condition-implication) | the landing page reveals the test as the answer | never lead the ad with "blood test" (commodity framing, drags perceived value + price down) |
| **Email sequences** | feeling-led subject + first line | the data and CTA in the back half, after the explanation lands | don't open a nurture email with a test pitch |
| **Social / video** | the feeling moment or question, fragment-dense | the reframe and reveal | don't open with credentials or a product |
| **Landing pages** | the feeling in the hero; the test as the route to the answer | markers grid + process below the fold | don't anchor the hero to "a blood test" as the product |

## 5. Current-state audit: what's already right, what changes

**Already right (do not touch):**
- The **prose voice is feeling-led.** Article vignettes open on the body-feel ("his head had gone cloudy", "tired all the time. Not sleepy. Flat."). tone-of-voice §7 already mandates "lead with the body-feel, not the marker." This is working.
- **Feeling hubs exist and perform the entry role:** `why-am-i-always-tired` (B hub), `brain-fog` (K hub), `how-to-increase-testosterone-naturally`.

**What changes:**

1. **Rebalance the portfolio toward feeling hubs.** Roughly half the live library targets commodity clinical terms (`ferritin-blood-test`, `crp-blood-test`, `fbc-blood-test`, `b12-blood-test`, `liver-function-blood-test`, `inflammatory-markers-blood-test`). These are legitimate as *spokes* (bottom-funnel capture, E-E-A-T, internal linking, the post-result "what does my number mean" reader). But they are **commodity, NHS-shadowed, price-compressed terms**, and the library is over-weighted on them. **Priority shifts to building the missing feeling hubs** — energy, sleep, stress, low mood, libido, weight/belly — which are the broad, uncontested, premium doors the demand data points to.

2. **Reframe the biomarker spokes.** Every clinical-term article must (a) bridge to the feeling within the first screen, and (b) link UP to its feeling hub. The definitional answer-box intro is fine for snippet capture, but it must resolve to the feeling/relief fast. `ferritin-blood-test` already does this ("the single most useful marker for unexplained fatigue"); the drier ones (`fbc`, `liver-function`) should follow that pattern.

3. **Acquisition entry must be feeling, never commodity-test.** Newsletter subjects, ad hooks, and email opens lead with the feeling. This is the biggest cross-channel change: do not let "blood test" or a biomarker be the thing that pulls a cold reader in. That framing invites a price-comparison against the NHS and £30 competitors.

4. **Stop chasing "blood test" commodity head terms for acquisition.** High volume, but contested, NHS-shadowed, and price-compressing. Own the feeling doors instead.

**Honest framing:** this is a *rebalance and a codification*, not a teardown. The voice is good. The change is topic-emphasis (more feeling hubs), spoke-framing (bridge to feeling faster), and a hard cross-channel rule that acquisition always enters through the feeling.

## 6. The pricing link (why this doc also sets the price ceiling)

Content strategy and the premium rate are the **same lever**:

- A funnel that enters via **commodity-clinical terms** ("blood test") anchors the customer to a £30 / free-NHS comparison. Your premium looks unjustified. Price ceiling drops.
- A funnel that enters via **feeling + explanation** ("why do I feel like this") has no commodity comparison. The customer is buying the answer and the relief. Price ceiling rises.

So: **the more feeling-led the funnel, the higher the price we can hold.** With supplement COGS proven cheap (~£8 real on the Daily Stack) and 66–77% margin headroom, the constraint on price is *perceived value*, and feeling-led, explanation-rich content is what raises it. This is the content-side justification for pricing to the wellness-outcome band (Vitl £34–36, Heights £42, AG1 £79), not the commodity-test band (Medichecks ~£30). See the pricing analysis for the £34.95 → £39.95 supplement test.

## 7. Quick reference

**Lead with (beat 1):** the feeling, in their words. Tired all the time. Brain fog. No energy. Can't switch off. Lost your drive. Carrying weight you didn't used to. Why does recovery take three days now.

**Never lead with:** a biomarker (CRP, ferritin, B12, testosterone level). "Blood test" / "panel" / "screening". A range or a number. A credential. A product. "Optimise your health."

**The test is the answer to the feeling. It is never the opening line.**
