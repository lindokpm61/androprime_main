## 1. The discoveries that move strategy

From the SEO/demand work ([portfolio-demand-gap-map.md](../seo-ai-search/portfolio-demand-gap-map.md), [discovery-symptom-first.md](../seo-ai-search/discovery-symptom-first.md), memory `project_seo_pillar_rebalance`):

1. **Demand is symptom-shaped, not product-shaped — and 2-3× bigger than the product-anchored view.** Men search how they feel ("always tired" 14,800; "brain fog" 14,800; "belly fat" ~40k; "male menopause" ~10k; "low sex drive"), not "testosterone test" or "hs-CRP". The whole funnel is named in product/clinical language the market doesn't use.
2. **Real underserved SERP gaps we can rank into cheaply:** inflammation/CRP (27,100, KD 11), liver (18,100, KD 18), metabolic (cholesterol/HbA1c ~12k), thyroid ("private thyroid test" KD 11). No UK men's-health specialist owns these.
3. **The terms we'd instinctively chase are the ones we can't win.** Testosterone/booster SERPs are owned by Numan/Manual. Compete on data-led credibility, not rank.
4. **The biggest demand maps to products we don't fully have yet** (CRP/inflammation, liver). Demand is arriving ahead of product.
5. **The moat isn't keywords.** It's the closed test→supplement loop + AI-citation (GEO/Pillar F) + Ewa's E-E-A-T + the founder brand. Keyword-chasing only reaches parity.

## 2. Decisions landed (this is the new posture)

### 2.1 Two-segment ICP split
"30 up to 65/70" is too wide to be one ICP. The social-media research (Section 4) shows it splits cleanly into two behaviourally different audiences that need different channels:

| | **Younger ~30-45** | **Older ~45-70** |
|---|---|---|
| Where | Instagram, some TikTok/Reddit, YouTube | **YouTube + Google search + Facebook** |
| Behaviour | Feed-discovery, follows influencers, short-form | **Searches/researches, long-form, does NOT buy off a feed** |
| Willingness to pay | Higher (more likely to go private) | **Lower / price-sensitive** |
| Best route | Short-form social + influencer/PT affiliate | **SEO + organic YouTube + Ewa-attributed trust (DTC)** |

The v4 GTM is organised around this split.

### 2.2 SEO + content + social re-tiered from afterthought to co-primary
Still **zero paid**. But owned organic content (search + social + YouTube) is the largest, cheapest, most defensible, and only *compounding* pool. It moves from Layer 5 to a co-primary engine alongside affiliate, resourced and sequenced from **pre-launch**, not "M1 onwards".

### 2.3 The content engine is one multi-channel spine, not an SEO project
One symptom pillar → **blog hub/spoke (SEO)** + **short-form social (Reels/Shorts/Facebook)** + **affiliate content kit (what PTs post)** + **email nurture hook**. Create once, atomise everywhere. Per segment: search + YouTube for older; short-form social + influencer for younger.

### 2.4 Social media is an owned channel (it currently isn't)
The locked plan treats social only as a surface affiliates post on, and **explicitly excludes organic TikTok**. Changes:
- **Split organic from paid.** Paid social stays excluded; organic is adopted.
- **Platform allocation by segment** (see 4.1), weighted to Facebook + YouTube for the older half, Instagram/short-form for the younger.
- **Rebuild the PT/influencer asset pack around symptom hooks**, not just personal-story/discount. This also fixes the recurring "what % of PTs actually posted" activation risk in the Week 3/6 gates — it's a content problem in disguise.

### 2.5 DTC is the only route to the older half
The affiliate model (PTs/gyms/influencers) reaches the younger, fitness-adjacent, already-active man. It does **not** reach the 55-70 sedentary man who's never been in a gym — and that man is arguably the highest clinical-need, best TRT-pipeline group. He is reachable only via owned **search + YouTube + trust**. So DTC is not optional; it is the only path to roughly half the stated market. Build the owned DTC engine **in parallel from day one**, not deferred to "M3, re-evaluate paid".

### 2.6 YouTube is the missing central channel for the older segment
Absent from the locked plan beyond an exclusion of YouTube *ads*. The data makes it central: 85% of 50-64s use it, 88% of over-55s weekly, male-majority, and it bridges *both* search intent and discovery. Organic YouTube (long-form explainers, "what your GP doesn't test", founder journey) is the platform + format + trust fit for the older searcher.

### 2.7 Trust model: Keith fronts, Ewa validates (no unconsented synthetic)
Ewa won't film. Resolved as:
- **Keith = on-camera face** (confirmed willing 2026-05-31). Long-form YouTube + the peer "bloke-my-age" framing the older audience responds to.
- **Ewa = clinical authority via a *consented, licensed digital twin* for short-form** (her approved scripts, her voice/likeness, signed licence). Suited to short scripted segments, NOT long-form (quality tells). Plus written attribution everywhere ("Clinically reviewed by Dr Ewa Lindo, GMC #4758565", Person schema).
- **No unconsented deepfake** — ruled out (compliance + brand-trust risk). Consent + per-script sign-off is the line that makes the twin legitimate.
- **Disclosure posture:** lean transparent (light "AI-generated from Dr Lindo's approved scripts" note); compliance to confirm. Trust-first brand should not let viewers later "discover" it.

### 2.8 Founder-journey content starts now, at baseline
Keith's own transformation (takes Kit 1 + Kit 3 Plus, shows real numbers, documents what shifts them) is the most persuasive *and* most compliant content the brand can make: genuine, peer, product-working-on-the-founder. **Do not gate on being camera-ready** — the before-state is the hook, and YouTube/SEO compound slowest, so starting late is the most expensive delay. Film the baseline now; cadence as results come in.

### 2.9 Pricing — hold premium above competitors, like-for-like (principle DECIDED; numbers to validate by test)

**Decided (evidence-backed direction):** price at a premium to *comparable* competitor products, **like-for-like** (our liver test vs their liver test; our hormone panel vs their hormone panel — never our narrow test vs their flagship). Premium varies in absolute terms by scope (a single-system test is cheaper than a 9-marker panel) but every rung sits above the equivalent competitor product. **Never enter the commodity floor.** Do **not** use a cheap entry kit as a DTC tripwire; lower entry *friction* with a free rung (content/quiz/email capture) + intent-matched products, not price.

- *Evidence (convergent):* the price-quality heuristic is strongest exactly when quality cues are absent (new brand, cold traffic, an unverifiable result) → in diagnostics, cheap signals low quality; premium brands suffer dilution from low-price entries; launch price anchors long-term and is hard to raise; the £29–49 standalone-liver / commodity band is a discount-driven Medichecks-owned lane (the demand map's "losing battleground"). Consistent with the locked positioning (marketing-plan §1: premium, above MediChecks). Sources logged in the 2026-05-31 pricing-research exchange.
- *Honest caveat:* the **direction** is evidence-based and convergent, but it is general/category evidence with one inferential leap (the diagnostic studies measure patients *choosing between providers*, not a cheap home-test brand converting worse), and there is **zero Andro-Prime-specific price-response data**. Category evidence lowers the risk; it does not remove it.

**To validate by test (NOT decided — these are hypotheses):** every specific number. The Liver Health Check is repriced from ~£45–55 to a **~£59–69 positioning target** (premium to the £29–49 competitor floor; still the lowest-ticket kit in the range *by scope, not by discount*). The number is a positioning target, not a costed or tested figure.

- *Test options:* (1) live A/B once DTC traffic exists — e.g. liver kit £59 vs £69 vs £89, measured on conversion × margin; (2) pre-launch **Van Westendorp** price-sensitivity survey to the waitlist/email list for an acceptable-price range before commitment. **VW survey is a flagged pre-launch action.**

**The liver kit is NOT a contradiction of "no cheap entry":** it is a *distinct, narrower product in a separate untapped lane* (the dilution-safe separate-product / sub-brand pattern), priced premium-to-category — not a discounted version of the flagship. It works as a DTC entry via **intent-match** ("liver function blood test" searchers convert on a liver kit because it's exactly what they wanted), not via low price.

## 3. Open decisions / flags (need Keith and/or Ewa)

1. **Entry-price tripwire — RESOLVED 2026-05-31 (rejected).** No cheap entry kit. Evidence (price-quality in diagnostics, dilution, anchoring, the commodity-floor competitive map) says hold premium; lower entry *friction* via a free rung (content/quiz/email) + intent-matched products, not price. The liver kit serves as the intent-matched lower-ticket entry, repriced premium-to-category. See §2.9. (The older segment's price-sensitivity showed up in *supplements*, a commodity; it does not safely transfer to a trust purchase like a blood test, where premium can reassure.) **Remaining sub-decision:** confirm the liver number via test (live A/B or Van Westendorp).
2. **Pricing sensitivity in the older segment.** Data: only 30% of over-55s would go private (vs 77% of 18-24s); supplement buying is cost-led (41%). Premium pricing may face *more* resistance in the older half, not less. Re-examine against v3 financial model.
3. **Product sequencing — RESOLVED 2026-05-31 (option b, Keith).** Build the content engine **now**; route by best-matched *live* product; redirect/insert to Kit 3 Plus and Liver as they come online on Keith's timeline. Execution principles (carry into the content-atomisation model):
   - Content is written about the **symptom/topic, evergreen** — never hard-wired to a specific kit. The body stays put; only the CTA target changes when the product does.
   - **CTAs route through one central component/config** so redirecting is a single change, not a per-article rewrite.
   - **Match intent to the best live product:** inflammation/CRP content → **Kit 2 now** (carries hs-CRP), upsell/redirect to **Kit 3 Plus** when live. Liver content → **email capture/waitlist now** (no live match), point to the **Liver kit** when launched. Do not route "liver function test" intent to a hormone kit (mismatch + misleading risk).
4. **Ewa digital-twin licence + disclosure + Pillar E.** Needs a proper likeness/voice licence (scope, duration, per-script approval, revocation). Pillar E (andropause/male-menopause umbrella, ~10-15k, low KD) is the highest-leverage narrative and is ASA/Ewa-gated — now a top-priority unlock, not a someday item.
5. **GA4 / consent dependency.** Not connected (memory `project_ga4_planned`). Without it, organic attribution is impossible. Hard prerequisite for measuring the content/DTC engine.
6. **FM-list constraint (already locked).** Content must not route readers into the FM list (CQC/ASA risk; memory `feedback_fm_list_not_in_content`). Symptom content routes to Kit 1/3/3 Plus only. The cold-to-warm bridge must capture into a *general* email nurture, not the FM list.

## 4. Supporting data — UK social media by age & behaviour (2025)

Pulled 2026-05-31 to test the "where do our customers live online" question. Confirmed the platform instinct, challenged the willingness-to-pay assumption, surfaced YouTube.

### 4.1 Platform by age
- **YouTube** — 85% of 50-64s; 88% of over-55s weekly; ~67% reach over-56; male-majority (~54%). Both search and discovery.
- **Facebook/Messenger** — dominates *time spent* for 35-54s (~50 min/day) and over-55s (62% daily reach, ~46 min/day). Older man's home turf.
- **TikTok** — ~83% under 35; only ~24% of UK users are 45+. Wrong platform for the older audience.
- **Instagram** — core 25-44; fades after. Younger segment only.
- **WhatsApp** — highest reach all ages but messaging, not an acquisition surface.

### 4.2 Behaviour (the key finding)
- Older consumers **search, they don't scroll-to-discover.** 84% of consumers use search engines for health answers (No. 1 channel).
- Only **17% of boomers discovered a product on social in the last 3 months; 4% ever bought via a social app.**
- Only **21% of over-65s** get health info from social (vs 52% of under-30s). Older barely follow influencers (4% of men 65+).
- Over-65s stick to familiar sites (36%), explore less.
- Implication: even on Facebook, the older man isn't converting off feed-discovery. Content for him should be informational/search-aligned, not interrupt-style.

### 4.3 Trust & willingness to pay
- **Trust matters** (supports the assumption): supplement buyers return to trusted/familiar brands (25%); most-trusted UK supplement brand earned it over 50 years → trust is *slow* to build for a new brand.
- **"Older don't mind paying" — NOT supported (flag):** only 30% of over-55s would use private healthcare next 12mo vs 77% of 18-24s; supplement buying led by cost (41%)/price (40%).
- **Men's help-seeking:** 24% wouldn't book an appointment; men engage at crisis. "Meet men where they are" (familiar, trusted settings) — supports both the trusted-intermediary (PT) model and the Ewa-credibility + Keith-peer approach.
